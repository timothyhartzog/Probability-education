The Cost of Rural Staffing

Critical Access Hospitals often spend 30-50% more per hour for **Locum Tenens** providers due to recruitment scarcity. However, the model show that investing in **Generalist Training** (e.g., ER physicians who can handle basic inpatient rounding) creates a "buffer" that stabilizes quality when permanent staff leave.

Survival Analysis and Clinician Retention

Clinician retention is a survival analysis problem. The survival function S(t) = P(employed at time t) and hazard function h(t) = lim_{Δt→0} P(leave in [t, t+Δt] | employed at t) characterize the departure process. The Cox proportional hazards model log h(t | x) = log h₀(t) + β₁x₁ + ... + β_p x_p estimates the effect of covariates (compensation, remote rural location, call burden, years in practice) on the hazard while allowing a flexible baseline hazard h₀(t). For rural physicians, the Kaplan-Meier estimator typically shows median retention of 4–6 years versus 8–10 years in urban settings — a difference with enormous cost implications given that recruiting a rural physician costs $150,000–$300,000.

Shift Scheduling as Integer Programming

Optimal nurse staffing is an integer programming (IP) problem: minimize total cost Σ_t Σ_s c_{s,t} x_{s,t} subject to: coverage constraints (Σ_s x_{s,t} ≥ d_t for each time period t), individual constraints (shift limits, rest requirements, hours per week), and fairness constraints (equitable weekend distribution). Solving large IP instances exactly is NP-hard, but practical hospital scheduling instances (100–500 nurses, 2-week horizon) can be solved with branch-and-bound in minutes using modern solvers. Stochastic scheduling models incorporate demand uncertainty: robust optimization finds schedules that are feasible across all demand realizations in a specified uncertainty set; stochastic programming minimizes expected cost across demand scenarios.

Queuing Networks and Provider Productivity

A healthcare facility is a queuing network: patients flow through registration → triage → physician → ancillary services → discharge, with each stage modeled as a queue. Jackson's theorem for open queuing networks (networks of M/M/c queues) gives that each queue in the network behaves independently at steady state, allowing analysis of bottlenecks. The bottleneck station with the highest utilization ρ_i = λ_i/(c_i μ_i) limits system throughput — adding capacity elsewhere provides no benefit until the bottleneck is relieved. Throughput analysis reveals that a rural ED spending 40% of physician time on documentation (a non-patient-care queue) effectively reduces clinical capacity by 40%, which is more tractable than hiring additional physicians.
