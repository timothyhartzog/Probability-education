Vaccine Impact and Herd Immunity

The SIRV Model

This simulator uses a modified SIRV (Susceptible → Infected → Recovered / Vaccinated) compartmental model. Vaccination moves individuals directly from Susceptible to a Vaccinated immune state, bypassing infection. The model tracks four compartments governed by differential equations:

  • dS/dt = −βSI/N − vS
  • dI/dt = βSI/N − γI
  • dR/dt = γI
  • dV/dt = vS

where β is the transmission rate, γ is the recovery rate, and v is the vaccination rate. The effective fraction susceptible decreases both through natural infection (S → R) and vaccination (S → V).

Herd Immunity Threshold

The basic reproduction number R0 is the average number of secondary infections from one case in a fully susceptible population. An epidemic grows when Reff = R0 · S/N > 1. The epidemic ends (herd immunity) when enough of the population is immune that Reff ≤ 1:

Ht = 1 − 1/R0

For measles (R0 ≈ 15), Ht ≈ 93% — nearly all of the population must be immune. For influenza (R0 ≈ 2–3), Ht ≈ 50–67%. For SARS-CoV-2 original strain (R0 ≈ 2.5), Ht ≈ 60%, but variants with higher R0 raised this threshold substantially.

Vaccine Efficacy and the Effective Reproduction Number

Vaccine efficacy (VE) is defined as 1 − relative risk: VE = 1 − (attack rate in vaccinated / attack rate in unvaccinated). A vaccine with 90% efficacy reduces the probability of infection by 90% per exposure. It does not mean 90% of vaccinated people are protected from every exposure — the actual protection depends on exposure dose, variant, and time since vaccination.

With vaccination coverage p and efficacy e, the effective reproduction number becomes: Reff = R0(1 − p·e). Setting Reff = 1 gives the vaccination coverage needed for herd immunity: p* = (1 − 1/R0) / e. Higher efficacy vaccines require lower coverage to achieve herd immunity.

Timing and Strategy — Mass vs. Ring Vaccination

Mass vaccination immunizes the general population as quickly as possible. Ring vaccination targets contacts of confirmed cases, creating a protective "ring" around each outbreak. Ring vaccination was central to the eradication of smallpox.

The timing of vaccination campaigns matters enormously. Vaccinating before a wave (pre-emptive) prevents cases entirely. Vaccinating during exponential growth competes with the epidemic — each day of delay allows the susceptible pool to shrink naturally but at the cost of preventable cases. The simulator lets you observe how the shape of the epidemic curve changes with vaccination rate and the timing of rollout.

Waning Immunity and Boosters

Vaccine-induced immunity and natural immunity both wane over time. If the rate of waning w is significant, vaccinated individuals return to the susceptible pool: an SIRVS model with transitions V → S. This creates endemic equilibrium even with vaccination — the pathogen circulates at low levels indefinitely rather than being eliminated.

The endemic equilibrium satisfies Reff = 1 with a steady-state susceptible fraction S* = 1/R0. Booster doses restart the immunity decay clock, maintaining high effective coverage. The optimal booster interval depends on the rate of waning and the cost of vaccination — a classic optimal stopping problem in public health.
