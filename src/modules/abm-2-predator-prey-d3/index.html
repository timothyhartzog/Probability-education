D3.js Predator-Prey Agent-Based Model

A pure D3.js predator-prey ecosystem simulator with three decision rule architectures — reactive, bounded rationality, and BDI — rendered in real time with Canvas agents and D3 SVG analytics. Observe Lotka-Volterra oscillations emerge from individual agent behavior.

Predator-Prey Dynamics and Agent Decision Architectures

Lotka-Volterra Equations

The classical Lotka-Volterra ODEs describe predator-prey population dynamics:

  • Prey: dN/dt = αN − βNP — prey grow at rate α, predation reduces them at rate βP
  • Predators: dP/dt = δNP − γP — predators gain from prey at rate δN, die at rate γ

The system has a conserved quantity (no dissipation), producing closed orbits in the phase plane. In the ABM, stochastic fluctuations and spatial effects cause the orbits to drift, potentially spiraling to extinction or to a noisy limit cycle, depending on population sizes and parameters.

Three Decision Rule Architectures

This simulation compares three architectures for agent decision-making:

  • Reactive: Stimulus-response only — agents react immediately to sensory input with fixed rules (e.g., flee if predator nearby). Fast and predictable but brittle.
  • Bounded Rationality: Agents evaluate a finite set of heuristic options and choose the locally best one. Captures satisficing behavior (Simon 1957): agents aim for "good enough" solutions rather than global optima.
  • BDI (Belief-Desire-Intention): Agents maintain internal state — beliefs about the world, desires (goals), and committed intentions (plans). The most cognitively realistic architecture, capable of planning, goal revision, and social coordination.
Emergent Dynamics and Phase Portrait

The phase portrait (predator count vs. prey count over time) reveals the qualitative dynamics: a stable limit cycle appears as a closed loop; a spiral inward means the system reaches equilibrium; a spiral outward or erratic path signals instability leading to extinction.

ABM predator-prey models show richer behavior than ODEs because: (1) demographic stochasticity can drive small populations to extinction; (2) spatial clustering allows prey to survive in refugia even when globally rare; (3) different decision architectures change the efficiency of predation, shifting the equilibrium point and the amplitude of oscillations.
