Bayesian Inference and Conjugate Priors

Bayes' Theorem as a Learning Rule

Bayesian inference treats the unknown parameter θ as a random variable. Before observing data, we encode our beliefs as the prior p(θ). After observing data x, Bayes' theorem updates this to the posterior:

p(θ | x) = p(x | θ) · p(θ) / p(x)

The likelihood p(x | θ) measures how probable the observed data is under each parameter value. The denominator p(x) = ∫ p(x|θ)p(θ)dθ is the marginal likelihood, a normalizing constant. As more data arrives, the posterior concentrates around the true θ — the data "overwhelms" the prior.

Conjugate Families

A prior is conjugate to a likelihood if the posterior is in the same parametric family as the prior. This gives closed-form updating rules.

  • Beta-Binomial: Beta(α, β) prior + Binomial(n, p) likelihood → Beta(α + k, β + n − k) posterior, where k successes are observed.
  • Gamma-Poisson: Gamma(α, β) prior + Poisson(λ) likelihood → Gamma(α + Σxi, β + n) posterior.
  • Normal-Normal: N(μ₀, τ₀²) prior + N(θ, σ²) likelihood (known σ) → Normal posterior with precision equal to the sum of prior and data precisions.

The Normal-Normal update has a beautiful form: the posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of prior mean and sample mean.

Credible Intervals vs Confidence Intervals

A 95% Bayesian credible interval [a, b] satisfies P(θ ∈ [a, b] | x) = 0.95 — there is a 95% probability the parameter lies in the interval, given the data. This is the natural interpretation most people mistakenly assign to frequentist confidence intervals.

A frequentist 95% confidence interval means: if we repeated the experiment many times, 95% of the intervals constructed by this procedure would contain the true θ. The parameter itself is fixed — it is the interval that is random. The two intervals often coincide numerically under flat priors but have fundamentally different interpretations.

Sequential Updating — Order Invariance

A key property of Bayesian inference: sequential updating gives the same posterior as batch updating. If you observe x1, then x2, the posterior after both observations equals the posterior from seeing both at once.

This is because the posterior after x1 serves as the prior for x2: p(θ|x1, x2) ∝ p(x2|θ) · p(θ|x1) ∝ p(x1|θ)p(x2|θ)p(θ). This is ideal for online learning, where data arrives one observation at a time.

Prior Choice and Sensitivity

The choice of prior matters most when data is scarce. With large n, the likelihood dominates and the posterior is insensitive to reasonable priors (Bernstein-von Mises theorem: the posterior is asymptotically normal around the MLE with variance equal to the inverse Fisher information).

Jeffreys priors are invariant under reparameterization: p(θ) ∝ √det I(θ), where I(θ) is the Fisher information matrix. They represent "uninformative" priors in a precise sense. For the Binomial with parameter p, the Jeffreys prior is Beta(½, ½) — a U-shaped distribution that is more diffuse than the uniform Beta(1,1).
