The Rural Demographic Paradox

Rural areas often face two simultaneous migration pressures: **Youth Out-Migration** (loss of tax base and caregivers) and **Amenity Migration** (urban retirees moving to scenic rural areas). This creates a "top-heavy" population pyramid with high clinical demand but shrinking support infrastructure.

Cohort-Component Population Projection

The cohort-component model projects population by tracking each age-sex cohort forward: P_{a+1,t+1} = P_{a,t} × (1 − m_a) × (1 − d_a) + N_{a+1,t}, where m_a is net out-migration rate at age a, d_a is mortality rate, and N represents in-migration. For rural areas, m_a is strongly age-dependent: youth (18–30) out-migrate for education and jobs, while retirees (65+) may in-migrate or stay. The Lee-Carter model extends this by decomposing mortality rates log(m_{a,t}) = α_a + β_a × κ_t, where κ_t is a time trend fit to historical data. This generates probabilistic mortality forecasts as κ_t follows a random walk.

Gravity Model of Migration and Push-Pull Factors

Migration flows between regions follow the gravity model: M_{ij} = k × P_i^α × P_j^β / d_{ij}^γ, where P_i and P_j are origin and destination populations, d_{ij} is distance, and k, α, β, γ are calibrated parameters. This mirrors Newton's law of gravitation — large populations "attract" migrants, distance "repels" them. Push factors for rural youth: limited job diversity, educational attainment mismatch, income differentials. Pull factors toward urban areas: higher wages (typically 30–50% premium), social network effects, amenity preferences. The wage gradient model predicts equilibrium migration equalizes real wages; deviations from equilibrium reflect information asymmetries, moving costs, and social capital.

Population Aging and Dependency Ratios

The old-age dependency ratio = (population 65+) / (population 20–64) measures the burden on the working-age population. Rural areas are aging faster than urban areas because of youth out-migration, not just differential mortality. A dependency ratio above 0.5 signals fiscal stress for local government and healthcare systems. The support ratio for healthcare specifically counts workers in health professions per elderly resident — rural areas with ratios below 1:10 face critical shortages. Scenario modeling with different migration and fertility assumptions reveals bifurcating futures: continued decline spirals (fewer workers → fewer services → more out-migration) versus revitalization pathways (remote work influx, retirement migration, healthcare investment).
