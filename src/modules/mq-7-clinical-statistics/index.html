Understanding Clinical Trial Statistics

Absolute vs Relative Risk

Relative Risk Reduction (RRR) sounds impressive but can be deeply misleading without context. A drug that reduces mortality from 0.002% to 0.001% has a 50% RRR — the same headline as one reducing mortality from 40% to 20%.

The Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR) tells the clinically meaningful story: the first drug saves 1 extra person per 100,000 treated; the second saves 20 per 100. Always ask: "50% reduction of what?"

Number Needed to Treat

The NNT answers the question: "How many patients must I treat to prevent one additional bad outcome?" It is simply 1/ARR.

NNT depends entirely on the baseline (control) risk. A treatment with a constant RRR of 25% yields NNT = 4 when CER is 100%, but NNT = 400 when CER is 1%. The lower the baseline risk, the more patients must be treated for one to benefit.

The NNH (Number Needed to Harm) is the analogous measure for adverse effects. A balanced clinical decision compares NNT with NNH.

Confidence Intervals

A 95% confidence interval does not mean "there is a 95% probability that the true value lies in this interval." The true value is fixed — it either is or is not in a given interval.

The correct interpretation: if we repeated the trial many times, about 95% of the resulting CIs would contain the true treatment effect. The CI simulation panel on the right illustrates this directly — each horizontal line is one simulated trial, and roughly 95% of the lines cross the true value.

Clinical vs Statistical Significance

A statistically significant result (p < 0.05) simply means the observed effect is unlikely under the null hypothesis. It says nothing about the magnitude or clinical importance of the effect.

Very large trials can detect tiny effects (ARR of 0.1%) with high statistical significance. Whether such an effect justifies the cost, side effects, and effort of treatment is a separate clinical judgment. Always examine the confidence interval and NNT, not just the p-value.
