Vaccine-Preventable Disease Modeling

1. The SEIRD Model for Vaccine-Preventable Diseases

This simulator uses the SEIRD (Susceptible-Exposed-Infectious-Recovered-Dead) compartmental model with vaccination. The susceptible pool is reduced by the fraction of the population that is effectively immunized:

Where the effective susceptible population accounts for vaccination: S0 = N × (1 − v × e), with v = vaccination coverage and e = vaccine efficacy.

2. R0 and the Herd Immunity Threshold

The basic reproduction number R0 determines how contagious a disease is in a fully susceptible population. The herd immunity threshold (HIT) is the minimum fraction of the population that must be immune to prevent sustained transmission:

For measles (R0 ≈ 12-18), HIT ≈ 92-95%. For polio (R0 ≈ 5-7), HIT ≈ 80-86%. This is why measles is the first disease to resurge when vaccination rates drop.

4. Disease Parameter Sources

All disease parameters in this module are drawn from peer-reviewed epidemiological literature and CDC surveillance data. Key parameters for each disease:

  • R0: Basic reproduction number — from systematic reviews and historical outbreak analyses
  • Incubation period: Time from exposure to symptom onset (1/σ)
  • Infectious period: Duration of contagiousness (1/γ)
  • Case fatality rate (CFR): Pre-vaccine era mortality rate
  • Vaccine efficacy: From clinical trial and real-world effectiveness data
  • Pre-vaccine annual cases: US estimates from CDC historical surveillance

6. Historical Context

Before vaccines, these diseases caused immense suffering in the United States. Measles infected 3-4 million Americans annually, killing 400-500 and causing thousands of cases of encephalitis. Polio paralyzed 15,000-20,000 children each year. Smallpox killed approximately 30% of those infected until its eradication in 1980. Diphtheria killed over 15,000 Americans annually in the early 1920s.

The CDC estimates that vaccines will prevent more than 21 million deaths and 732 million cases of disease among children born in the last 20 years in the United States alone (CDC, 2014). Globally, WHO estimates vaccines prevent 3.5-5 million deaths every year.
