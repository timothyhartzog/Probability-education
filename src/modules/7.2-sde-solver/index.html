Stochastic Differential Equations

What is an SDE?

A stochastic differential equation (SDE) has the form:

dXt = μ(Xt, t) dt + σ(Xt, t) dBt

The drift μ governs the deterministic tendency of the process; the diffusion coefficient σ governs the amplitude of random fluctuations. The dBt term is formal notation for Gaussian white noise — the Brownian increment over an infinitesimal time step.

An SDE solution is a stochastic process Xt satisfying the integral form Xt = X0 + ∫μ ds + ∫σ dB. Existence and uniqueness are guaranteed under Lipschitz growth conditions on μ and σ (the Itô existence theorem).

Geometric Brownian Motion — The Black-Scholes Model

GBM has the form dXt = μXt dt + σXt dBt. Applying Itô's formula to log(Xt), the exact solution is:

Xt = X0 exp((μ − σ²/2)t + σBt)

The log of Xt is normally distributed — Xt is lognormal. The mean grows as X0eμt but the median (most likely path) grows as X0e(μ−σ²/2)t. The term −σ²/2 is the Itô correction: higher volatility actually lowers the typical path due to Jensen's inequality applied to the log.

Ornstein-Uhlenbeck — Mean Reversion

The OU process dXt = θ(μ − Xt) dt + σ dBt describes mean-reverting dynamics. The parameter θ > 0 is the speed of reversion, μ is the long-run mean. The exact solution is:

Xt = μ + (X0 − μ)e−θt + σ ∫₀ᵗ e−θ(t−s) dBs

The stationary distribution is N(μ, σ²/2θ). OU processes model interest rates (Vasicek model), temperatures, and any quantity that fluctuates around an equilibrium. The half-life of deviations is log(2)/θ.

Euler-Maruyama Numerical Method

The Euler-Maruyama scheme discretizes the SDE as:

Xt+Δt = Xt + μ(Xt, t)Δt + σ(Xt, t)√Δt · Z

where Z ~ N(0,1). This is the stochastic analogue of the Euler method for ODEs. It has strong order ½ (pathwise error ~ √Δt) and weak order 1 (distributional error ~ Δt). For better pathwise accuracy, the Milstein scheme adds a correction term: + ½σσ'((√Δt·Z)² − Δt), achieving strong order 1.

Cox-Ingersoll-Ross — Non-Negative Processes

The CIR process dXt = κ(θ − Xt) dt + σ√Xt dBt is used to model interest rates and volatility because Xt ≥ 0. The Feller condition 2κθ ≥ σ² ensures the process never hits zero.

The diffusion coefficient σ√Xt vanishes at zero, creating a reflecting boundary. The stationary distribution is a scaled Gamma distribution: X∞ ~ Gamma(2κθ/σ², σ²/2κ). This is the basis of the Heston stochastic volatility model in quantitative finance.
