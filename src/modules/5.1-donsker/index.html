About Donsker's Theorem

Donsker's Invariance Principle

Donsker's theorem (1951) is a functional extension of the Central Limit Theorem. It states that the rescaled random walk converges in distribution to Brownian motion as a random element of the space of continuous functions C[0,1].

The key insight: any random walk with i.i.d. mean-zero, variance-one steps produces the same limiting process, regardless of the step distribution. This universality is visible in the visualization above.

The distribution of sup0≤t≤1 Bt is the folded normal: its CDF is P(M ≤ x) = 2Φ(x) - 1 for x ≥ 0, and its density is f(x) = 2φ(x) = (2/√(2π)) e-x²/2. The histogram of simulated suprema should converge to this curve.

Step Distributions

All four distributions have mean 0 and variance 1, as required by the theorem:

  • Symmetric Bernoulli: X = +1 or -1 each with probability 1/2
  • Standard Normal: X ~ N(0,1)
  • Centered Uniform: X ~ Uniform(-√3, √3), giving variance = 1
  • Centered Exponential: X = Exp(1) - 1, giving mean 0 and variance 1

Despite their very different shapes, all produce Brownian motion in the limit.
