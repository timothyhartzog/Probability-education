About Donsker's Theorem

Donsker's Invariance Principle

Donsker's theorem (1951) is a functional extension of the Central Limit Theorem. It states that the rescaled random walk converges in distribution to Brownian motion as a random element of the space of continuous functions C[0,1].

The key insight: any random walk with i.i.d. mean-zero, variance-one steps produces the same limiting process, regardless of the step distribution. This universality is visible in the visualization above.

Step Distributions

All four distributions have mean 0 and variance 1, as required by the theorem:

  • Symmetric Bernoulli: X = +1 or -1 each with probability 1/2
  • Standard Normal: X ~ N(0,1)
  • Centered Uniform: X ~ Uniform(-√3, √3), giving variance = 1
  • Centered Exponential: X = Exp(1) - 1, giving mean 0 and variance 1

Despite their very different shapes, all produce Brownian motion in the limit.

Weak Convergence in C[0,1] and the Skorokhod Representation

Donsker's theorem is a statement about convergence in distribution of random elements of a function space. The space C[0,1] of continuous functions on [0,1], equipped with the sup-norm topology, is a Polish space, so Prokhorov's theorem applies: a sequence of probability measures on C[0,1] is tight (relatively compact) if and only if it is uniformly tight — meaning the paths are collectively equicontinuous with high probability.

The scaled random walk Bn satisfies this tightness condition: the increments are small (order 1/√n) and independent, preventing large oscillations with high probability. Combined with convergence of finite-dimensional distributions to those of Brownian motion (which follows from the CLT), this gives the full weak convergence.

The Skorokhod representation theorem is a key tool: if Xn ⟶d X in a Polish space, there exist random variables X̃n ⟶a.s. X̃ on a common probability space, where each X̃n has the same law as Xn and X̃ has the same law as X. This lets you pass from distributional convergence to almost-sure convergence for the purposes of proving limit theorems for functionals.

Empirical Process Theory and the Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test

Donsker's theorem is the foundation of empirical process theory. Given i.i.d. observations X1, ..., Xn with CDF F, the empirical CDF is Fn(x) = (1/n) Σ 1{Xi ≤ x}. The scaled process √n (Fn(x) − F(x)) converges weakly (as a function of x) to a Brownian bridge B(F(x)), where B is a Brownian bridge on [0,1].

This gives the asymptotic distribution of the Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic: Dn = supx |Fn(x) − F(x)|. By Donsker and the CMT, √n · Dn ⟶d supt∈[0,1] |B(t)| (the Kolmogorov distribution). This allows the KS test's critical values to be computed analytically.

More generally, empirical process theory studies √n (Pfn − Pf) = (1/√n) Σ (f(Xi) − Ef(X)) for classes of functions f. The Glivenko-Cantelli theorem (strong law for function classes) and Donsker classes (classes for which the empirical process satisfies a CLT) are central to the theory of statistical learning and semiparametric efficiency.
