About Meta-Analysis

Forest Plots

A forest plot is the standard graphical display of a meta-analysis. Each study is shown as a row: the square marks the point estimate (sized proportional to the study's weight), and the horizontal line shows its confidence interval. The pooled estimate is shown as a diamond at the bottom, whose width indicates the confidence interval of the combined effect.

The vertical line of no effect (OR = 1 or RD = 0) is the key reference. If a study's CI crosses this line, its result is not statistically significant at the chosen confidence level. If the pooled diamond does not cross the line, the overall meta-analytic result is statistically significant.

Fixed vs Random Effects

A fixed-effect model assumes all studies estimate the same underlying true effect. The Mantel-Haenszel method is preferred for sparse binary data; inverse variance weighting is more general.

A random-effects model (DerSimonian-Laird) allows the true effect to vary between studies. It incorporates between-study variance (τ²) into the weights, giving small studies relatively more influence. When heterogeneity is low, fixed and random models converge.

Heterogeneity

I² quantifies the percentage of variability due to true differences rather than chance: <25% is low, 25–75% moderate, >75% high. Cochran's Q tests whether observed differences exceed sampling error (significant p means real heterogeneity exists). τ² is the estimated between-study variance on the effect-size scale.

The prediction interval shows where 95% of future study effects would fall — it is always wider than the confidence interval and conveys the clinical relevance of heterogeneity better than I² alone.

Publication Bias

Funnel plots plot effect size against precision (1/SE). In the absence of bias, studies scatter symmetrically around the pooled estimate. Asymmetry (typically a gap in the lower-left) suggests that small negative studies were not published.

Egger's regression tests for funnel asymmetry formally. The trim-and-fill method imputes "missing" studies to restore symmetry and re-estimates the pooled effect, providing a sensitivity analysis.
