About Markov Chains

Definition

A Markov chain is a stochastic process where the future state depends only on the current state, not on the history. Formally, for states X0, X1, ...:

The transition matrix P encodes all transition probabilities: entry Pij is the probability of moving from state i to state j in one step.

Stationary Distribution

A distribution π is stationary if it satisfies πP = π. Under certain conditions (irreducibility, aperiodicity), the chain converges to π regardless of the initial distribution.

Watch the distribution evolution panel: the bars converge to the dashed overlay as n increases. The total variation distance measures how far the current distribution is from stationarity.

Convergence and Mixing

The mixing time is the number of steps needed for the chain to be close to its stationary distribution. Chains with absorbing states or periodic structure may not converge to a unique stationary distribution.

Try the different presets to see how chain structure affects convergence: the weather model converges quickly, the periodic chain oscillates forever, and the gambler's ruin gets absorbed.

Irreducibility, Aperiodicity, and the Perron-Frobenius Theorem

A Markov chain converges to a unique stationary distribution if and only if it is:

  • Irreducible: every state can be reached from every other state in a finite number of steps. No isolated "islands."
  • Aperiodic: no state is only revisited at regular intervals (the GCD of return times equals 1). The periodic preset demonstrates failure: the chain oscillates between sets and π is not a pointwise limit, even though it is the time-average distribution.

The Perron-Frobenius theorem guarantees that for an irreducible aperiodic chain, the transition matrix P has a unique eigenvalue of modulus 1 (which is 1 itself), and all other eigenvalues satisfy |λ| < 1. The stationary distribution is the corresponding left eigenvector. The second-largest eigenvalue |λ2| determines the spectral gap and hence the mixing rate: the TV distance decays geometrically as |λ2|n.

Absorbing States and Gambler's Ruin

A state is absorbing if once entered, it cannot be left. For a chain with absorbing states, the long-run behavior is characterized by: (1) the probability of being absorbed into each absorbing state, and (2) the expected time to absorption.

The Gambler's Ruin is the classic example: a gambler starts with $k, gains $1 or loses $1 at each step with probabilities p and 1-p, and stops at $0 (bankrupt) or $N (wins). The probability of ruin starting from $k is:

P(ruin | start at k) = ((q/p)k - (q/p)N) / (1 - (q/p)N) when p ≠ q

At p = 0.5 (fair game): P(ruin) = 1 - k/N. With even a slight house edge (p < 0.5), ruin is nearly certain for large N. The absorbing chain in the dashboard shows how the probability mass concentrates at the absorbing states over time.

Detailed Balance and Reversibility

A Markov chain is reversible with respect to distribution π if it satisfies detailed balance: πi Pij = πj Pji for all i, j. Detailed balance says the probability flux from i to j equals the flux from j to i in stationarity.

Reversibility implies π is the stationary distribution, but the converse is false: a chain can have a unique π without being reversible (think of a directed cycle). Reversible chains have real eigenvalues, making their spectral analysis tractable. The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm (Module 8.2) is designed to produce reversible chains with a prescribed target distribution.

The Kolmogorov criterion: a chain is reversible if and only if for every cycle of states i0, i1, …, ik = i0, the product of transition probabilities in one direction equals the product in the reverse direction.
