Laws of Large Numbers

The Weak and Strong Laws

Both laws concern the running average Sn/n = (X1 + … + Xn)/n of i.i.d. random variables with mean μ.

  • Weak Law (WLLN): For any ε > 0, P(|Sn/n − μ| > ε) → 0. The probability of being far from μ vanishes — convergence in probability.
  • Strong Law (SLLN): P(limn→∞ Sn/n = μ) = 1. Almost every sample path converges — a much stronger statement.

The SLLN requires only that E[|X|] < ∞ (finite first moment). The WLLN can hold under even weaker conditions. Neither requires finite variance — but without finite variance, the running average can oscillate dramatically before settling.

Convergence Rate — √n Fluctuations

The error Sn/n − μ shrinks at rate 1/√n. More precisely, by the CLT, √n (Sn/n − μ) converges in distribution to N(0, σ²). This means the fluctuations of the running average scale as σ/√n.

On the log-log convergence rate chart you should see the error track a slope of −½: doubling n reduces the error by a factor of √2 ≈ 1.41. For distributions with infinite variance (Cauchy, Pareto α ≤ 2), the running average never converges because E[X] does not exist — the "average" itself is undefined.

Heavy Tails — When the LLN Breaks Down

The Cauchy distribution has no finite mean. Its running average does not converge — it continues to fluctuate wildly because each new extreme observation shifts the average dramatically. A single large observation can dominate the sum.

Distributions with power-law tails P(X > x) ∝ x−α have finite mean only when α > 1 and finite variance only when α > 2. The simulator lets you explore this boundary: near α = 1 or α = 2, convergence is slow and erratic even with many samples.

Borel-Cantelli and Almost Sure Convergence

The proof of the SLLN for bounded variables uses the Borel-Cantelli lemma: if Σ P(An) < ∞, then P(An infinitely often) = 0. Applying Chebyshev's inequality and summing over suitable subsequences gives P(|Sn/n − μ| > ε i.o.) = 0.

Etemadi's proof (1981) obtains the full SLLN from E[|X|] < ∞ alone, without requiring finite variance or independence beyond pairwise independence. The key insight is to work along the subsequence ⌊αk⌋ and interpolate.

Ergodic Theory — LLN Beyond Independence

The SLLN extends far beyond i.i.d. sequences via ergodic theory. A measure-preserving transformation T on a probability space (Ω, F, P) is ergodic if the only T-invariant sets have measure 0 or 1. The Birkhoff Ergodic Theorem then guarantees that for any integrable f:

(1/n) Σk=0n-1 f(Tkω) → E[f] almost surely

The i.i.d. SLLN is the special case where T is the left shift on the sequence space. But the ergodic theorem covers far more: stationary Markov chains (where it gives the time-average = π-average result), deterministic chaotic systems (where time averages converge to Lebesgue integrals), and stationary processes in signal processing. The key is that ergodicity rules out the existence of "traps" that the system never escapes.

LLN in Statistics — Consistency and the Glivenko-Cantelli Theorem

In statistics, the LLN is the foundation of consistency: an estimator θ̂n is consistent if θ̂n →p θ as n → ∞. The sample mean is consistent for E[X]; the sample variance is consistent for Var(X); any continuous function of consistent estimators is consistent (CMT).

The Glivenko-Cantelli theorem is the LLN for the empirical CDF: supx |Fn(x) − F(x)| → 0 almost surely. This "uniform LLN" guarantees that the entire empirical distribution converges to the true distribution, not just its mean. It underlies the validity of empirical plug-in estimators and non-parametric methods.

The law of large numbers for Monte Carlo: if X1, ..., Xn are i.i.d. draws from a distribution with density p, then (1/n) Σ g(Xi) → Ep[g(X)] almost surely for any integrable g. This justifies Monte Carlo integration as a universal numerical method — the only requirement is that the expectation exists.
