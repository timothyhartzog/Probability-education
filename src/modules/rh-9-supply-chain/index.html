The Zero-Inventory Problem

Critical Access Hospitals (CAHs) cannot economically store rare blood types or expensive biologics. This creates a **Zero-Inventory Trap** where a single trauma patient can deplete the regional stock. **Drone networks** (Zipline-style) transform this linear logistical problem into a spatial network problem, effectively "expanding" the pharmacy radius of a hub hospital.

Inventory Models — EOQ and Safety Stock

The Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) model minimizes total annual cost = holding cost + ordering cost: EOQ = √(2DS/H), where D is annual demand, S is ordering cost per order, and H is annual holding cost per unit. In rural healthcare, S includes both monetary ordering cost and the "emergency premium" for urgent resupply of critical items. Safety stock = z_{α} × σ_d × √L buffers against demand uncertainty (σ_d = demand standard deviation per period) and lead time L, where z_α is the z-score for service level α. A rural pharmacy targeting 99% service level (z = 2.33) with weekly demand σ = 5 units and 2-week lead time needs safety stock ≈ 2.33 × 5 × √2 ≈ 16.5 units — significant for a low-volume facility.

Last-Mile Logistics and the Vehicle Routing Problem

The Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) asks: given a depot and n customer locations with known demands, find minimum-cost delivery routes for a fleet of vehicles with capacity constraints. VRP is NP-hard (the traveling salesman problem is a special case). For rural healthcare, the VRP captures the trade-off between infrequent bulk deliveries (lower transport cost, higher inventory cost) and frequent small deliveries (lower inventory, higher transport). Time-window VRP adds constraints that deliveries occur within specified windows (e.g., cold-chain medications must arrive within 4 hours of refrigeration departure). Drone delivery changes the objective function: drones have lower per-mile cost and no road network constraints but are limited to small payloads and affected by weather.

Stochastic Demand and the Newsvendor Problem

The newsvendor model determines the optimal order quantity q* when demand D is random and unsold inventory is wasted (or salvaged at a loss): q* = F^{-1}(c_u / (c_u + c_o)), where c_u is the underage cost (lost sale or patient harm), c_o is the overage cost (waste or holding), and F^{-1} is the inverse CDF of demand. The critical ratio c_u/(c_u + c_o) is the optimal service level. For critical medications (c_u >> c_o), the target service level exceeds 99%; for perishable non-critical supplies, 85–90% may be optimal. The newsvendor is the fundamental model for vaccine ordering, blood product management, and seasonal medication stocking — each setting has a different cost ratio and demand distribution (Poisson for vaccines, log-normal for blood products).
