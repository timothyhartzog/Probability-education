The Operational Formula: Little's Law

In hospital operations, the **Fundamental Relation** is Little's Law. It states that, under steady-state conditions, the long-term average number of items (L) in a stationary system is equal to the long-term average effective arrival rate (λ) multiplied by the average time (W) that an item spends in the system.

Rural Impact: In Critical Access settings, where the bed capacity (C) is extremely low (often 25), a utilization rate exceeding 80% exponentially increases the probability of "blocking" (when there is no bed for an incoming patient), even if the average occupancy remains within theoretical limits.

Erlang's Loss Formula and Target Occupancy

The Erlang B formula gives the probability that an arriving patient finds all beds occupied (blocking probability): B(c, a) = (a^c / c!) / Σ_{k=0}^{c} (a^k / k!), where a = λ/μ is the offered load (arrivals × mean length of stay) and c is the number of beds. Healthcare regulators often target 85% occupancy, but this is a compromise: at 85%, blocking probability is low for large hospitals but unacceptably high for small ones with c = 10–20 beds. A 10-bed unit at 85% occupancy has a blocking probability near 15%, meaning 1 in 6 patients would be turned away. Optimal capacity planning requires specifying an acceptable blocking probability (e.g., 5%) and solving for c given the demand distribution.

Length of Stay Distributions and Mixture Models

Hospital length of stay (LOS) is not exponentially distributed despite Markovian arrival assumptions. LOS distributions are right-skewed with heavy tails — a minority of patients ("super-utilizers") have extreme LOS values that dominate bed-days. The log-normal and Weibull distributions fit LOS better than exponential, but the best fit is often a mixture model: a mixture of two log-normal components captures short-stay (same-day, 1–3 days) and long-stay (complex or social patients, 7+ days) subpopulations separately. Ignoring this structure leads to systematic underestimation of bed requirements — models calibrated on median LOS miss the tail that drives bed-block and delayed discharges.

Seasonal Demand Cycles and Time-Series Forecasting

Inpatient demand follows predictable seasonal patterns: winter respiratory illness peaks (typically 20–40% above summer baseline), summer trauma and mental health crises, and holiday discharge suppression causing bed-block in early January. Decomposition separates demand into trend, seasonal, and irregular components. The SARIMA model (Seasonal ARIMA) with period 52 (weeks) or 12 (months) captures autocorrelated demand and seasonal cycles simultaneously. For operational planning, 7-day rolling forecasts with prediction intervals guide staffing decisions; for capital planning, 5–10 year projections require demographic trend models. Forecast uncertainty compounds over the planning horizon — 95% PI width approximately doubles for each doubling of the forecast horizon.
