Agent-Based Modeling

What is an Agent-Based Model?

An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational simulation where autonomous agents follow local rules, and macro-level patterns emerge from their collective interactions. Unlike equation-based models (ODEs, PDEs) that describe population-level averages, ABMs track each individual and their interactions explicitly.

ABMs are especially powerful when: heterogeneity matters (agents differ), spatial structure is important, nonlinear feedback loops exist, or the system has discrete units (people, animals, firms) that don't behave like continuous quantities.

Epidemic ABM — SEIRD vs. ODE Models

The epidemic ABM implements an SEIRD model (Susceptible → Exposed → Infected → Recovered/Deceased) at the individual level. Transmission occurs when an infectious agent is within the infection radius of a susceptible one, with probability β per contact per step — a contact process.

The key difference from ODE models: the ABM captures stochastic extinction (outbreaks that fizzle before spreading), spatial clustering (clusters of unvaccinated individuals enabling local spread), and superspreaders (high-contact individuals who drive disproportionate transmission). All of these are invisible in mean-field ODE compartmental models.

Flocking (Reynolds' Boids Algorithm)

Craig Reynolds (1987) showed that three simple local rules produce realistic flocking behavior without any central coordination:

  • Separation: Avoid crowding — steer away from neighbors within a close range.
  • Alignment: Match velocity — steer toward the average heading of neighbors.
  • Cohesion: Stay together — steer toward the average position of neighbors.

This is a canonical example of emergence: complex collective behavior (coordinated flocking, milling, V-formations) arising entirely from local rules with no global blueprint. The same algorithm models fish schools, pedestrian crowds, and distributed robotic swarms.

Schelling Segregation Model

Thomas Schelling (1971) showed that even mild individual preferences for same-type neighbors produce extreme macro-level residential segregation. An agent is "happy" if at least a threshold fraction t of its neighbors share its type. Unhappy agents move to random empty cells.

For t ≈ 0.3 (agents only want 30% similar neighbors — not even a majority), the system converges to a nearly fully segregated state. This is a profound lesson about the fallacy of composition: individual preferences that seem mild produce aggregate patterns that are far more extreme than any individual intends. The final segregation index depends nonlinearly on t, with a sharp transition around a critical threshold.

Predator-Prey — Lotka-Volterra from Agent Rules

The predator-prey ABM implements the Lotka-Volterra system at the individual level. Prey eat grass and reproduce; predators hunt prey and starve if unfed. The population dynamics exhibit the classic Lotka-Volterra oscillations: prey peak first, then predators peak as they benefit from abundant prey, causing prey to collapse, causing predator starvation, allowing prey to recover.

The ABM differs from the ODE in key ways: populations can go extinct (ODE cycles never do), individual variability creates amplitude drift, and spatial structure enables coexistence even when mean-field theory predicts extinction. The phase portrait (predator vs. prey populations) reveals whether the system spirals inward, outward, or maintains a stable cycle.
