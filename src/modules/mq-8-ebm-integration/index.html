Understanding Evidence-Based Medicine

What Is EBM?

Evidence-Based Medicine (EBM) is the conscientious, explicit, and judicious use of current best evidence in making decisions about the care of individual patients. Coined by Gordon Guyatt and popularized by David Sackett, EBM integrates three fundamental components:

  • Best External Evidence — from systematic research
  • Clinical Expertise — the clinician's accumulated experience and skills
  • Patient Values — the unique preferences and expectations each patient brings

None of these components alone is sufficient. Without evidence, practice risks becoming outdated. Without expertise, evidence may be misapplied. Without patient values, medicine becomes paternalistic.

The Evidence Hierarchy

Not all evidence is created equal. The hierarchy of evidence ranks study designs by their susceptibility to bias:

  1. Systematic Reviews & Meta-Analyses — synthesize multiple studies, highest quality
  2. Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) — gold standard for causal inference
  3. Cohort Studies — follow groups over time, observational
  4. Case-Control Studies — compare groups retrospectively
  5. Case Series & Case Reports — descriptive, hypothesis-generating
  6. Expert Opinion — lowest in hierarchy but still valuable when evidence is absent

The GRADE framework (Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development and Evaluation) provides a structured approach to rating evidence quality and strength of recommendations.

The 5 Steps of EBM

The practice of EBM follows a systematic five-step process:

  1. ASK — Formulate a clear clinical question using the PICO framework (Patient/Problem, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome)
  2. ACQUIRE — Search for the best available evidence (PubMed, Cochrane Library, clinical guidelines)
  3. APPRAISE — Critically evaluate the evidence for validity, impact, and applicability
  4. APPLY — Integrate evidence with clinical expertise and patient values to make a decision
  5. ASSESS — Evaluate the outcome and refine the approach
Shared Decision Making

Shared decision making (SDM) is the process by which clinicians and patients work together to make healthcare decisions that align with the best evidence and the patient's values. SDM is the practical manifestation of EBM's integration principle.

Key elements include: presenting options with balanced information about benefits and harms, using decision aids when available, eliciting patient preferences and values, and reaching a mutually agreed-upon plan. SDM is particularly important when there is clinical equipoise or when outcomes depend heavily on patient preferences (e.g., quality vs. quantity of life tradeoffs).
