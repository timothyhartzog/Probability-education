Itô Calculus and Stochastic Integration

Why Ordinary Calculus Fails for Brownian Motion

Brownian motion Bt has infinite total variation on any interval — the sum of |Bti+1 − Bti| diverges as the partition is refined. This means the Riemann-Stieltjes integral ∫ f dB cannot be defined path-by-path in the classical sense.

Instead, the Itô integral is defined as a limit in mean square: ∫₀ᵀ f(t) dBt = lim Σ f(ti)(Bti+1 − Bti), where f is evaluated at the left endpoint of each interval. This left-endpoint rule makes the integral an honest martingale and gives it zero expectation when f is adapted.

Quadratic Variation — [B]t = t

The quadratic variation of Brownian motion is [B]t = t. This is the fundamental fact of stochastic calculus: Σ (ΔB)² → t as mesh → 0. Because ordinary smooth functions have zero quadratic variation, this extra "dt" term is invisible in classical calculus but crucial here.

Heuristically, (dBt)² = dt. This is why Itô's formula has an extra second-derivative term that classical chain rules do not. The quadratic variation chart shows the running sum of (ΔB)² tracking the diagonal line y = t.

Itô's Formula — The Stochastic Chain Rule

For a smooth function g and Brownian motion Bt, Itô's formula states:

dg(Bt) = g'(Bt) dBt + ½ g''(Bt) dt

The extra ½ g'' dt term arises from quadratic variation. For example, g(x) = x²: d(Bt²) = 2Bt dBt + dt, so Bt² − t is a martingale. Without the correction term, you would incorrectly write d(B²) = 2B dB and conclude Bt² is a martingale — but E[Bt²] = t ≠ 0.

Itô vs Stratonovich Convention

The Stratonovich integral uses the midpoint rule: ∫ f ∘ dB = lim Σ f((ti+ti+1)/2)(ΔB). It satisfies the classical chain rule — the stochastic correction term cancels. The conversion between conventions is: f ∘ dB = f dB + ½ f' dt.

Itô is preferred in probability theory (adapted integrands, martingale property). Stratonovich appears naturally in physics and geometry (e.g., Riemannian manifolds, limits of smooth noise). When modeling real physical systems with colored noise that becomes white in a limit, the Stratonovich convention typically gives the correct limiting SDE.

Isometry and Martingale Properties

The Itô isometry is E[(∫₀ᵀ f dB)²] = ∫₀ᵀ E[f²] dt. This is the key identity used to compute variances of stochastic integrals and to prove the integral is well-defined. It mirrors the L² norm identity for ordinary integrals but holds in expectation rather than path-by-path.

The Itô integral Mt = ∫₀ᵗ f dB is a local martingale whenever f is adapted and square-integrable. Under the additional condition E[∫ f² dt] < ∞ it is a true martingale with E[Mt] = 0 for all t.

Girsanov's Theorem — Changing the Measure

Under the physical measure P, Brownian motion Bt has zero drift. Girsanov's theorem says we can define a new measure Q under which B̃t = Bt + ∫₀ᵗ θs ds is a Brownian motion, provided the Novikov condition E[exp(½ ∫ θs² ds)] < ∞ holds. The measure change is given by the Radon-Nikodym derivative (the Girsanov exponential martingale): dQ/dP = exp(−∫ θs dBs − ½ ∫ θs² ds).

In finance, Girsanov is used to move from the real-world measure P (where assets grow at their expected return μ) to the risk-neutral measure Q (where all assets grow at the risk-free rate r). The drift shift θ = (μ − r)/σ is the market price of risk. Under Q, the discounted asset price is a martingale, and option prices equal expected discounted payoffs.

More generally, Girsanov's theorem is the stochastic calculus version of the likelihood ratio between two diffusion models. It quantifies how much a drift change costs in terms of probability mass: the KL divergence between Q and P equals ½ EP[∫ θs² ds]. This connects to the Donsker-Varadhan large deviations rate function and makes Girsanov the continuous-time analog of exponential tilting in importance sampling.
