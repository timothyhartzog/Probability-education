Central Limit Theorem

The Theorem and What It Says

Let X1, X2, … be i.i.d. random variables with mean μ and finite variance σ². Define the standardized sum:

Zn = (Sn − nμ) / (σ√n)

The CLT states that Zn converges in distribution to N(0,1) as n → ∞. Crucially, this holds regardless of the shape of the underlying distribution — heavy-tailed, skewed, discrete, or continuous — as long as the variance is finite.

The CLT does not say the individual Xi become normal. It says the rescaled sum of many independent contributions approaches Gaussian shape.

Rate of Convergence — Berry-Esseen Theorem

The Berry-Esseen theorem quantifies how quickly the CLT kicks in. If E[|X|³] < ∞ (finite third moment), then:

supx |Fn(x) − Φ(x)| ≤ C ρ / (σ³ √n)

where ρ = E[|X − μ|³] and C ≈ 0.4748. The convergence rate is O(1/√n). For symmetric distributions the skewness term vanishes and convergence is faster — effectively O(1/n). This is why a coin-flip sum looks normal much sooner than a heavily skewed distribution.

When the CLT Fails — Heavy Tails

If the variance is infinite (e.g., Cauchy distribution, Pareto with α ≤ 2), the CLT does not apply. Instead, appropriately normalized sums converge to a stable distribution with heavier-than-Gaussian tails.

The Cauchy is the most famous example: the average of n Cauchy(0,1) random variables is itself Cauchy(0,1), so the distribution never changes shape no matter how many samples you average — the standard 1/√n normalization does nothing. The Lévy-Khintchine representation characterizes all possible limit laws for sums of i.i.d. variables.

Q-Q Plots as a Diagnostic Tool

A quantile-quantile (Q-Q) plot compares the empirical quantiles of Zn against the theoretical quantiles of N(0,1). If the points lie on the diagonal y = x, the distribution is normal.

Deviations reveal the character of the underlying distribution: S-shaped curves indicate light tails; points curving upward at both ends indicate heavy tails; asymmetric deviations indicate skewness. Even with large n, a heavy-tailed parent distribution produces noticeable bow-out in the Q-Q plot because the extreme order statistics are far from Gaussian.

Practical Uses — Confidence Intervals and Hypothesis Tests

Nearly every standard statistical procedure exploits the CLT. The 95% confidence interval x̄ ± 1.96 σ/√n works because x̄ is approximately normal. The z-test and t-test both rely on the sample mean being approximately normal.

The key practical question is: how large does n need to be? As a rough rule: n ≥ 30 suffices for mildly skewed distributions; n may need to be in the hundreds for highly skewed or discrete distributions. Multivariate CLT and the Cramér-Wold Device

For random vectors Xi ∈ ℝd with mean μ and covariance matrix Σ, the multivariate CLT states that √n (X̄n − μ) →d N(0, Σ). The limiting distribution is a multivariate Gaussian with the same covariance structure as the summands.

The Cramér-Wold device reduces the multivariate CLT to the univariate case: a sequence of random vectors converges in distribution to a multivariate Gaussian if and only if every linear combination tTXn converges in distribution to a univariate Gaussian N(0, tTΣt). This is both theoretically elegant and practically useful — testing convergence of linear combinations is sufficient.

The multivariate CLT underlies the asymptotic theory of maximum likelihood estimators: under regularity conditions, √n(θ̂MLE − θ) →d N(0, I(θ)−1), where I(θ) is the Fisher information matrix. This gives simultaneous confidence ellipsoids for all parameters, and the Wald test statistic n(θ̂ − θ0)TI(θ̂)(θ̂ − θ0) →d χ²(d).
