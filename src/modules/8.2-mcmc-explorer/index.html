Markov Chain Monte Carlo

Why MCMC? The Curse of Dimensionality

Exact Bayesian inference requires evaluating the normalizing constant p(x) = ∫ p(x|θ)p(θ) dθ. In high dimensions, this integral is intractable. A d-dimensional grid with 100 points per dimension requires 100d evaluations — 10200 for d = 100.

MCMC circumvents this by constructing a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target posterior p(θ|x). We run the chain long enough to explore the posterior, then treat the chain's samples as approximate draws from it — all without computing the normalizing constant.

Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm

Given current state θt, the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm proposes a move θ* ~ q(·|θt) and accepts it with probability:

α = min(1, [π(θ*)q(θt|θ*)] / [π(θt)q(θ*|θt)])

where π is the unnormalized target. If the proposal is symmetric (q(a|b) = q(b|a)), this simplifies to α = min(1, π(θ*)/π(θt)). The acceptance criterion ensures detailed balance: the chain satisfies π(a)P(a→b) = π(b)P(b→a), guaranteeing π is stationary.

Burn-in, Thinning, and Mixing

Burn-in refers to the initial samples discarded while the chain moves from its starting state toward the target distribution. The chain converges to stationarity after sufficient burn-in, but "sufficient" is hard to define rigorously.

Thinning keeps every k-th sample to reduce autocorrelation between successive samples. While it reduces effective sample size, it lowers storage requirements and the samples look more like independent draws.

Mixing describes how quickly the chain explores the target distribution. A poorly mixing chain gets stuck in local regions. The effective sample size (ESS) equals n / (1 + 2Σk≥1 ρk), where ρk is the lag-k autocorrelation. ESS much less than n signals poor mixing.

Optimal Proposal Scaling

For a Gaussian random-walk proposal in d dimensions, the optimal acceptance rate is approximately 23.4% (Roberts, Gelman, Gilks 1997). Targeting this rate via adaptive scaling of the proposal variance gives near-optimal mixing.

Too small a proposal σ: almost every proposal is accepted, but the chain moves slowly (small steps, high autocorrelation). Too large a σ: proposals often land in low-probability regions and are rejected, leaving the chain stuck. The 23.4% rule balances these: the proposal σ should scale as 2.38/√d times the posterior standard deviation.

MCMC Convergence Diagnostics

Unlike optimization, MCMC has no convergence criterion that can be checked exactly. Diagnostics can detect non-convergence but cannot prove convergence.

  • Trace plots: should look like white noise around a stable mean, with no trends or long runs at extreme values.
  • ACF (autocorrelation function): should decay rapidly to zero. Slow decay indicates high serial correlation and poor mixing.
  • Gelman-Rubin R̂ statistic: compares within-chain and between-chain variance across multiple parallel chains. Values near 1.0 (< 1.1 conventionally) indicate convergence.
Hamiltonian Monte Carlo — Gradient-Guided Sampling

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) introduces auxiliary momentum variables p ∈ ℝd and simulates Hamiltonian dynamics H(θ, p) = −log π(θ) + pTp/2. The position θ evolves by following the gradient of log π, which allows proposals that are far from the current point but still accepted with high probability. The acceptance rate of well-tuned HMC approaches 65–80%, versus ~23% for random-walk Metropolis in high dimensions.

HMC scales dramatically better than random-walk proposals in high dimensions: random walk requires O(d) steps to traverse the target, while HMC requires O(d1/4) steps. This is the algorithm underlying Stan and modern Bayesian workflow. The No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) automatically tunes the trajectory length by detecting when the simulation starts to "U-turn" back toward its start — eliminating the need to tune the number of leapfrog steps by hand.

HMC requires a differentiable log-posterior, making it ideal for continuous parameters but inapplicable to discrete latent variables. For discrete structures, alternatives include Gibbs sampling (exact conditional sampling), slice sampling, or reparameterization tricks that embed discrete variables in a continuous space.
