The Gravity Model of Patient Flow

In rural systems, patients do not always go to the nearest hospital. Their choice is dictated by Gravity: a function of the facility's weight (quality/services) and the geographic friction (travel time).

2-Step Floating Catchment Area (2SFCA): This model calculates a ratio of beds-to-population for every facility, then sums these ratios for every town based on travel thresholds. It allows us to identify regions where demand far exceeds supply, even if a facility is "nearby."

Two-Step Floating Catchment Area — The Mathematics

The 2SFCA method computes accessibility in two steps. Step 1: for each supply location j, calculate the supply-to-demand ratio R_j = S_j / Σ_{k: d_{kj} ≤ d_0} D_k, where S_j is supply (beds/providers), D_k is demand (population) at each location k within threshold d_0. Step 2: for each demand location i, sum the ratios of all supply locations within the threshold: A_i = Σ_{j: d_{ij} ≤ d_0} R_j. The result is an accessibility score where higher values indicate better access. The threshold d_0 is a critical modeling choice — too small and rural residents appear unserved; too large and urban facilities dilute rural access scores.

Distance Decay and the Gravity Model

The basic 2SFCA uses a hard distance threshold, but real patient behavior follows distance decay: the probability of using a facility decreases continuously with travel time, not abruptly at a cutoff. The enhanced 2SFCA (E2SFCA) replaces the binary weight with a decay function W(d): Gaussian W(d) = e^{−d²/β²}, exponential W(d) = e^{−βd}, or inverse power W(d) = d^{−β}. The gravity model P_{ij} = A_j^α / d_{ij}^β (analogous to Newton's gravity) weights destination attractiveness A_j and penalizes distance. Calibrating β from actual patient origin data is essential — rural populations show different decay rates than urban ones, often willing to travel farther for specialized care.

Equity and the Lorenz Curve of Access

Geographic access inequity can be measured with the same tools as income inequality. Plotting cumulative population (sorted by access score) against cumulative access gives a Lorenz curve; the Gini coefficient of access = (area between Lorenz curve and diagonal) / 0.5 ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (all access concentrated in one location). Rural areas typically have Gini coefficients for healthcare access above 0.4, versus 0.2–0.3 in urban areas. The Theil index is a related decomposition: total inequality = within-region inequality + between-region inequality, allowing attribution of access gaps to geography vs. facility distribution decisions.
