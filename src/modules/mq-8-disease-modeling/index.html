Mathematical Foundations of Epidemic Modeling

1. Compartmental Models (Kermack-McKendrick, 1927)

The foundational work by Kermack and McKendrick (1927) established the mathematical framework for epidemic modeling using ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The population is divided into compartments representing disease states, and transition rates between compartments capture the dynamics of disease spread.

Key Parameters:

  • Beta (transmission rate): Product of contact rate (c) and transmission probability per contact (p). beta = c * p
  • Gamma (recovery rate): Inverse of the average infectious period. gamma = 1/D where D is the duration of infectiousness
  • R0 (basic reproduction number): Average number of secondary infections from a single infected individual in a fully susceptible population. R0 = beta/gamma
  • Herd immunity threshold: 1 - 1/R0. The proportion of the population that must be immune to prevent sustained transmission

2. The SIR Model and Epidemic Threshold

The SIR model demonstrates a fundamental result: an epidemic can only occur when R0 > 1. When R0 < 1, the disease dies out. This epidemic threshold theorem provides the theoretical basis for vaccination programs — if enough people are vaccinated to push Reff below 1, the epidemic cannot sustain itself.

3. SEIR Model — Incorporating Latency

Many diseases have an incubation period where individuals are infected but not yet infectious. The SEIR model adds an Exposed (E) compartment. The rate sigma (1/incubation period) governs transition from E to I. This is critical for diseases like COVID-19 (incubation ~5.2 days), Ebola (~8-10 days), and measles (~10-14 days).

4. SIS Model — Endemic Diseases

For diseases that do not confer lasting immunity (e.g., gonorrhea, common cold), the SIS model allows recovered individuals to become susceptible again. This produces endemic equilibria where the disease persists indefinitely in the population at a steady-state prevalence of I* = 1 - 1/R0.

5. SEIRS Model — Waning Immunity

When immunity wanes over time (as with seasonal coronaviruses or influenza), the SEIRS model adds a rate xi governing the loss of immunity. This produces oscillatory dynamics and recurrent epidemic waves, matching the observed seasonality of many respiratory infections.

6. Agent-Based Models (ABMs)

While compartmental models assume a well-mixed population, agent-based models (ABMs) simulate individual agents with heterogeneous properties, spatial locations, and contact patterns. ABMs can capture:

  • Spatial heterogeneity: Disease spreads locally, creating spatial clusters
  • Individual variation: Age, immunity, behavior differences
  • Superspreader events: A small fraction of infected individuals cause disproportionate transmission (80/20 rule observed in SARS, COVID-19)
  • Stochastic effects: Important when case counts are small (early/late epidemic, or small populations)
  • Complex interventions: Contact tracing, targeted quarantine, ring vaccination

Notable ABM frameworks include EpiSimdemics (Barrett et al., 2008), FRED (Grefenstette et al., 2013), and Covasim (Kerr et al., 2021) used extensively for COVID-19 policy analysis.

7. Network Epidemiology

The structure of contact networks profoundly affects epidemic dynamics. Key network types include:

  • Erdos-Renyi Random Networks: Homogeneous degree distribution. Epidemic threshold depends on mean degree.
  • Watts-Strogatz Small-World: High clustering with short path lengths. Models social networks with local contacts and occasional long-range links. Accelerates epidemic spread compared to lattices.
  • Barabasi-Albert Scale-Free: Power-law degree distribution with hubs. Highly vulnerable to epidemic spread (very low epidemic threshold) but also highly responsive to targeted vaccination of hubs.
  • Lattice Networks: Regular grid contacts. Disease spreads as a spatial wave front.

Pastor-Satorras and Vespignani (2001) showed that on scale-free networks, the epidemic threshold vanishes — even diseases with very low transmissibility can spread. This contrasts sharply with random networks where a clear threshold exists.

9. Intervention Modeling

Interventions modify the effective reproduction number Reff:

  • Vaccination: Reff = R0 * (1 - vaccine_coverage * vaccine_efficacy). Reduces the susceptible pool.
  • Social distancing: Reduces the contact rate c, thereby reducing beta. A 50% contact reduction halves Reff.
  • Quarantine/isolation: Removes infectious individuals from the mixing population, reducing the effective infectious period.
  • Contact tracing: Identifies and isolates exposed individuals before they become infectious, breaking transmission chains.
