About Rural Epidemiology Methods

SEIR/SEIRD Compartmental Models

Compartmental models divide a population into Susceptible, Exposed, Infectious, Recovered, and optionally Dead compartments. The ODE system is:

dS/dt = −βSI/N
dE/dt = βSI/N − σE
dI/dt = σE − γI
dR/dt = γ(1−μ)I
dD/dt = γμI

This simulator uses 4th-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) with dt = 0.5 days for numerical accuracy. RK4 takes a weighted average of four slope estimates within each step, achieving O(h⁴) local error — far superior to Euler's O(h) method for stiff epidemic curves.

R₀, Rₑ, and the Herd Immunity Threshold

The basic reproduction number R₀ = β/γ is the expected number of secondary cases from one infectious individual in a fully susceptible population. When R₀ > 1, an epidemic can grow; when R₀ < 1, it cannot sustain.

The effective reproduction number Rₑ = R₀(1−p) accounts for prior immunity fraction p (vaccination + natural immunity). The epidemic is controlled when Rₑ < 1, requiring p > 1 − 1/R₀ — the Herd Immunity Threshold (HIT).

The final attack rate r satisfies the implicit equation r = 1 − e^(−R₀·r), solved here by fixed-point iteration. For R₀ = 3, the final attack rate is ~94% in a fully susceptible population.

Attack Rate Analysis and Outbreak Investigation

In a foodborne outbreak, the attack rate (AR) is the proportion of exposed individuals who become ill. The Risk Ratio (RR) = AR(exposed) / AR(unexposed) measures the association between a food item and illness. RR ≥ 3 is considered strong evidence of a vehicle; RR near 1 suggests no association.

The Attributable Fraction (AF) = (RR − 1)/RR estimates the proportion of cases in exposed persons attributable to that food. The Odds Ratio (OR) = (a·d)/(b·c) approximates RR when attack rates are low.

This tool identifies the probable vehicle as the food with the highest RR. In rural settings with small outbreak sizes, Fisher's exact test is preferred over chi-square for significance testing due to small cell counts.

Diagnostic Test Performance and Bayes' Theorem

Sensitivity = TP/(TP+FN) — probability a diseased person tests positive (true positive rate). Specificity = TN/(TN+FP) — probability a non-diseased person tests negative (true negative rate).

Positive Predictive Value (PPV) depends critically on prevalence via Bayes' theorem: PPV = (Sens × Prev) / (Sens × Prev + (1−Spec) × (1−Prev)). In rural low-prevalence settings, even a highly specific test yields low PPV — a major source of false-positive burden.

Likelihood Ratios (LR+ = Sens/(1−Spec), LR− = (1−Sens)/Spec) are prevalence-independent and express the diagnostic shift. LR+ ≥ 10 is a strong rule-in; LR− ≤ 0.1 is a strong rule-out.

Rate Instability and Poisson Confidence Intervals

In rural populations with small denominators, rate instability is the primary statistical challenge. The CDC standard: rates based on fewer than 20 events should be suppressed or flagged due to excessive statistical noise.

This tool uses the Byar approximation for Poisson 95% CI: lower = n·(1 − 1/(9n) − 1.96/√(9n))³, upper = (n+1)·(1 − 1/(9(n+1)) + 1.96/√(9(n+1)))³. The relative CI width = (upper−lower)/point_estimate × 100% is the key stability metric.

Multi-year pooling is the standard remedy: pooling 3–5 years of events increases effective n, narrowing CIs. The YPLL (Years of Potential Life Lost before age 75) captures premature mortality burden, giving greater weight to deaths in younger age groups.

NNT, ARD, and Clinical Significance

The Number Needed to Treat (NNT) = 1/ARD where ARD = Risk(control) − Risk(treatment) is the Absolute Risk Difference. NNT is the number of patients who must receive treatment to prevent one additional adverse outcome. NNT = 1 means every treated patient benefits; NNT = 100 means 100 must be treated to benefit one.

When the treatment group has higher risk than control, the same formula gives Number Needed to Harm (NNH). ARD is more clinically meaningful than Relative Risk (RR) or Odds Ratio (OR) because it is sensitive to baseline risk — a 50% relative risk reduction means very different things at 2% vs 40% baseline risk.
