Understanding Diagnostic Test Probability

Bayes' Theorem in Medicine

Bayes' theorem tells us how to update the probability of disease given a test result. The Positive Predictive Value (PPV) answers: "If I test positive, what is the probability I actually have the disease?"

The denominator represents all positive results — both true positives from diseased individuals and false positives from healthy individuals. When prevalence is low, even a tiny false-positive rate can overwhelm the true positives.

The Base Rate Fallacy

The base rate fallacy (or base rate neglect) is the tendency to ignore prevalence when interpreting a test result. Consider: a test with 95% sensitivity and 95% specificity sounds impressive. But if the disease prevalence is only 1%:

  • In 1,000 people: 10 have disease, 990 are healthy
  • True positives: 10 × 0.95 = 9.5 ≈ 10
  • False positives: 990 × 0.05 = 49.5 ≈ 50
  • PPV = 10 / (10 + 50) = 16.7%

So ~83% of positive results are false alarms. This is why mass screening for rare diseases requires extremely specific tests, and why confirmatory testing is essential.

Likelihood Ratios

Likelihood ratios express how much a test result changes the odds of disease. They are independent of prevalence, making them portable across populations:

LR+ = Sensitivity / (1 − Specificity) — how much more likely a positive result is in a diseased vs. healthy person. LR+ > 10 is a strong positive test.

LR− = (1 − Sensitivity) / Specificity — how much less likely a negative result is in a diseased vs. healthy person. LR− < 0.1 is a strong negative test.

Post-test odds = Pre-test odds × LR. This is the odds form of Bayes' theorem and is the basis of bedside Bayesian reasoning.

Clinical Decision Making

In clinical practice, the key insight is that pre-test probability matters enormously. A positive mammogram in a 40-year-old woman (low prevalence) has very different implications than the same result in a woman with a palpable mass (high pre-test probability).

Sequential testing leverages this: the post-test probability after the first test becomes the pre-test probability for the second test. This is why clinicians order confirmatory tests — each successive test refines the probability estimate.

Screening programs must balance sensitivity (catching all cases) against the psychological and financial costs of false positives. The Number Needed to Test (1/PPV) indicates how many positive results must be worked up to find one true case.
