Geometric Brownian Motion

The foundational model for stock price dynamics:

The solution is log-normal: S(t) = S₀ exp[(μ − σ²/2)t + σW(t)], where W(t) is a standard Wiener process. This implies returns are normally distributed but prices are always positive.

Black-Scholes Formula

The closed-form solution for European option pricing:

Key assumptions: constant volatility, no dividends (generalized with q), continuous trading, no transaction costs, risk-neutral pricing.

The Greeks

Partial derivatives measuring option sensitivity:

Delta (Δ) measures price sensitivity, Gamma (Γ) the rate of delta change, Vega (ν) volatility sensitivity, Theta (Θ) time decay, and Rho (ρ) interest rate sensitivity.

Portfolio Theory (Markowitz)

Mean-variance optimization for an N-asset portfolio:

The efficient frontier is the set of portfolios achieving maximum return for a given risk level. The tangency portfolio maximizes the Sharpe ratio (μ_p − r_f)/σ_p.

Value at Risk (VaR) & CVaR

VaR gives the maximum loss at a confidence level over a time horizon. CVaR (Expected Shortfall) is the expected loss given that the loss exceeds VaR — it is a coherent risk measure unlike VaR.

Interest Rate Models

Vasicek: mean-reverting with possible negative rates. Analytical bond pricing.
CIR: Square-root diffusion ensures non-negative rates. Both are single-factor short-rate models. The Feller condition 2κθ > σ² prevents zero being reached in CIR.

Merton Jump-Diffusion

Extends GBM with Poisson-driven jumps of random log-normal magnitude. Captures fat tails, volatility smiles, and sudden crashes that GBM cannot reproduce. λ is the jump intensity, J ~ N(μ_J, σ_J²) is the jump size in log-space.

Stochastic Processes in Finance — Key Concepts

The Fundamental Tension: Return vs. Risk

All of quantitative finance rests on one observation: higher expected returns require accepting higher risk. The Sharpe ratio (μ − rf)/σ measures risk-adjusted return — how much excess return you earn per unit of volatility. No model can reliably produce high Sharpe ratios because any persistent alpha is arbitraged away in efficient markets.

The Itô correction term −σ²/2 in GBM is the mathematical manifestation of this tension: for a fixed drift μ, higher volatility σ reduces the median path even as it widens the distribution. Compounding amplifies losses asymmetrically — a 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover.

No-Arbitrage and Risk-Neutral Pricing

The Black-Scholes framework is built on a no-arbitrage principle: any two assets with identical payoffs must have identical prices. By constructing a delta-hedged portfolio (long option, short Δ shares) that is instantaneously riskless, the drift μ drops out of the pricing equation, leaving only the risk-free rate r.

This is the risk-neutral measure Q: under Q, all assets grow at the risk-free rate, and option prices equal EQ[payoff · e−rT]. The real-world probability measure P determines what actually happens; Q is a mathematical tool for pricing. The Girsanov theorem transforms between the two by shifting the drift of Brownian motion.

Volatility — Implied vs. Historical

Historical volatility is estimated from past returns: σ̂ = std(daily log-returns) × √252. Implied volatility is backed out from observed option prices by inverting Black-Scholes. The two differ because options markets price future expected volatility, which includes a risk premium.

The volatility smile (implied vol varies with strike K) shows that Black-Scholes's constant-σ assumption is violated in practice: out-of-the-money puts are more expensive than BS predicts (crash risk premium). Jump-diffusion models and stochastic volatility models (Heston) reproduce the smile by allowing the volatility itself to be random or allowing sudden jumps.

Coherent Risk Measures — CVaR vs. VaR

Value at Risk (VaR) at level α is the loss threshold exceeded with probability 1 − α. It fails a key property of coherent risk measures: it is not sub-additive — the VaR of a portfolio can exceed the sum of individual VaRs, perversely penalizing diversification.

CVaR (Conditional VaR or Expected Shortfall) is the expected loss given that the loss exceeds VaR. It is sub-additive (diversification never increases CVaR), convex, and monotone — all properties of coherent risk measures (Artzner et al. 1999). Regulators under Basel III now require CVaR for internal model capital requirements.
