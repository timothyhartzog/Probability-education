About Convolution via Characteristic Functions

The Product Rule

For independent random variables X and Y, the characteristic function of their sum satisfies φX+Y(t) = φX(t) · φY(t). This is the Fourier analog of convolution: multiplication in frequency domain corresponds to convolution in the spatial domain.

CLT in Fourier Domain

For i.i.d. Xi with mean 0 and variance 1, the CF of the standardized sum is [φ(t/√n)]n. Taylor expanding φ(t) = 1 - t²/2 + o(t²), we get [1 - t²/(2n) + ...]n → e-t²/2, which is the CF of N(0,1).

Universality

The CLT holds regardless of the summand distribution (given finite variance) because the Taylor expansion of any CF matches the Gaussian CF to second order. Higher-order terms are suppressed by 1/√n, making the product converge to e-t²/2.

The remarkable consequence: the only information from X that survives in the limit is its mean μ and variance σ². All the detail about skewness, kurtosis, and tail shape is washed out as n → ∞. This is what makes the CLT so universal and why the Gaussian distribution appears throughout science.

Convolution vs. Characteristic Functions — When to Use Each

The density of X + Y (when densities exist) is the convolution fX+Y(z) = ∫ fX(x) fY(z-x) dx. This is direct but computationally expensive for multiple variables (the density of a sum of n variables involves n-fold convolution).

Characteristic functions reduce n-fold convolution to a product: φX1+…+Xn(t) = ∏ φXi(t). Inversion then recovers the density if needed. This is the key reason CFs are preferred in theoretical proofs: products are algebraically simpler than nested integrals.

In practice: for sums of Normals, Poissons, or Gammas (all closed under convolution), use the known closed-form result. For arbitrary mixtures, numerical CF inversion (Gil-Pelaez formula) is efficient. For a small fixed n, direct convolution via FFT is often fastest.

Cumulants and the Role of Higher Moments

The cumulant generating function K(t) = log φ(t/i) has coefficients called cumulants κn = K(n)(0). Key properties:

  • κ1 = E[X] (mean)
  • κ2 = Var(X) (variance)
  • κ3 = E[(X-μ)3] (raw skewness)
  • κ4 = E[(X-μ)4] - 3σ4 (excess kurtosis numerator)

Crucially, cumulants are additive for independent variables: κn(X+Y) = κn(X) + κn(Y). For the Gaussian, all cumulants of order ≥ 3 are zero — which is the characteristic property. The rate at which the CLT approaches normality (Berry-Esseen, Edgeworth expansion) is controlled by the ratio κ3/(σ√n), the standardized skewness of the sum.

Application — Portfolio Risk and the Normal Power Approximation

In quantitative finance, the loss distribution of a portfolio of n assets is a sum of (correlated) random variables. The characteristic function approach handles this efficiently: φL(t) = E[eitL] where L = ∑i wi Xi.

For uncorrelated assets, the CLT guarantees approximate normality for large n — this is the foundation of the normal VaR model. For correlated assets or fat-tailed returns, the CF is modified using the Lévy-Khintchine representation, leading to the normal power approximation or stable distribution models that capture the non-Gaussian tails observed in practice.
