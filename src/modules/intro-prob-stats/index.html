Kolmogorov's Axiomatic Framework

Kolmogorov Axioms

A probability space is a triple (Ω, ℱ, P) where Ω is the sample space, ℱ is a σ-algebra of events, and P is a probability measure satisfying:

Axiom 1 (Non-negativity): P(A) ≥ 0 for all A ∈ ℱ.
Axiom 2 (Normalization): P(Ω) = 1.
Axiom 3 (σ-additivity): For disjoint A₁, A₂, … ∈ ℱ, P(⋃ Aᵢ) = Σ P(Aᵢ).

These three axioms entail all classical rules: P(∅) = 0, P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A), the inclusion-exclusion formula, and monotonicity.

Independence

Events A and B are independent if and only if P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B), equivalently P(A | B) = P(A). Independence is a symmetrical property: A independent of B implies B independent of A. Pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence — the classic counterexample is the Bernstein coins construction. In the Venn diagram, independence corresponds to the intersection area equalling the product of the two circle areas relative to Ω.

Random Variables & Expectation

PMF vs PDF

A discrete random variable X has a probability mass function (PMF) p(k) = P(X = k) ≥ 0 with Σₖ p(k) = 1. A continuous random variable has a probability density function (PDF) f(x) ≥ 0 with ∫ f(x) dx = 1. Crucially, for a continuous RV P(X = x) = 0 for every single point — probabilities only make sense over intervals: P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx.

Expected Value

The expected value (mean) is the probability-weighted average of all possible values:

E[X] is a linear operator: E[aX + bY] = aE[X] + bE[Y] always, with no independence assumption. The Law of the Unconscious Statistician (LOTUS): E[g(X)] = Σ g(k) p(k) or ∫ g(x) f(x) dx, without needing the distribution of g(X) itself.

Variance & Standard Deviation

Variance measures spread around the mean:

The computational shortcut Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])² is often easier. Standard deviation σ = √Var(X) has the same units as X. Key properties: Var(aX + b) = a² Var(X) (constant shifts don't affect spread), and for independent X, Y: Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y). Note this additivity fails for dependent variables — a crucial point in portfolio theory and error propagation.

Moment Generating Functions

The MGF M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] encodes all moments via M_X^{(k)}(0) = E[X^k]. The MGF uniquely identifies the distribution (when it exists in a neighborhood of 0), and the MGF of a sum of independent RVs is the product of their MGFs. The characteristic function φ_X(t) = E[e^{itX}] always exists, making it more general — it is the Fourier transform of the PDF and plays a central role in proving the CLT.

Limit Theorems

Normal Distribution & Empirical Rule

The normal distribution N(μ, σ²) is the ubiquitous bell curve:

The empirical rule: approximately 68% of mass lies within 1σ, 95% within 2σ, and 99.7% within 3σ of the mean. This is not approximate for the normal — these are exact consequences of the erf function. The standard normal Z ~ N(0,1) is obtained by Z = (X − μ)/σ.

Weak vs Strong Law of Large Numbers

Let X₁, X₂, … be i.i.d. with E[|X|] < ∞ and mean μ. The Weak LLN states that for every ε > 0, P(|X̄ₙ − μ| > ε) → 0 as n → ∞ (convergence in probability). The Strong LLN (Kolmogorov) strengthens this: P(X̄ₙ → μ) = 1 (almost sure convergence). The key distinction is that a.s. convergence controls an uncountable family of events simultaneously, while weak convergence only pins down marginal probabilities. Almost sure convergence implies convergence in probability, but not vice versa.

Statistical Inference

Estimators: Bias, Efficiency, Consistency

An estimator T(X₁, …, Xₙ) is a function of the sample used to estimate a parameter θ. Key properties:

  • Unbiasedness: E[T] = θ. X̄ is unbiased for μ; the biased variance estimator S²ₙ = (1/n)Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)² has E[S²ₙ] = (n−1)/n · σ².
  • Efficiency: Among unbiased estimators, the one with smallest variance. The Cramér-Rao lower bound gives the minimum achievable variance: Var(T) ≥ 1/I(θ), where I(θ) is the Fisher information.
  • Consistency: T → θ in probability as n → ∞. The LLN guarantees X̄ is consistent for μ.
Degrees of Freedom & Bessel's Correction

The unbiased sample variance uses n−1 in the denominator (Bessel's correction): S² = (1/(n−1)) Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)². The reason: the deviations Xᵢ − X̄ lie in an (n−1)-dimensional subspace (they sum to zero), so there are only n−1 free pieces of information. Using n−1 makes E[S²] = σ² exactly. The n−1 is the number of degrees of freedom, a recurring concept in χ² and t distributions.

Confidence Interval Interpretation

A 95% CI does not mean "there is a 95% probability that μ lies in this interval." The parameter μ is fixed (frequentist view); the interval is random. The correct interpretation: if we repeated the sampling procedure many times, 95% of the constructed intervals would contain μ. The visualization above shows exactly this — resample and observe that approximately 95% of the drawn bands cover the true mean. The CI width scales as σ/√n, so quadrupling n halves the margin of error.

Bernstein-von Mises Theorem

In a Bayesian framework with a prior π(θ) and likelihood L(θ | xₙ), the Bernstein-von Mises theorem (also called the Bayesian CLT) states that under regularity conditions, the posterior distribution concentrates around the MLE and is asymptotically normal: π(θ | xₙ) → N(θ̂_MLE, I(θ₀)⁻¹/n). This provides a philosophical reconciliation: as n → ∞, Bayesian credible intervals and frequentist confidence intervals coincide — the choice of prior becomes asymptotically irrelevant, and both frameworks yield the same inference.

Hypothesis Testing & the t-Test

Null and Alternative Hypotheses

Hypothesis testing is framed as a decision between two competing claims about a parameter θ. The null hypothesis H₀ is the default claim (typically "no effect," μ = 0). The alternative hypothesis H₁ is the research claim. A one-sample t-test evaluates whether a sample mean x̄ is inconsistent with H₀: μ = μ₀. The test is two-tailed when H₁: μ ≠ μ₀ (evidence in either direction counts), one-tailed when H₁: μ > μ₀ or μ < μ₀.

P-value Interpretation

The p-value is the probability, under H₀, of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one computed. It is NOT the probability that H₀ is true. Formally: p = P(|T_{df}| ≥ |t_obs| | H₀). We reject H₀ at level α if p < α. The cutoff α = 0.05 is a convention, not a law — it means accepting a 5% false positive rate. A p-value just below 0.05 is not meaningfully different from one just above it: the p-value is a continuous measure of evidence, not a binary classifier.

Type I and Type II Errors

There are two kinds of errors: rejecting H₀ when it is true (Type I error, rate = α), and failing to reject H₀ when it is false (Type II error, rate = β). The power of a test is 1 − β. There is an inherent tradeoff: decreasing α (requiring stronger evidence to reject) increases β. Power depends on the effect size (μ − μ₀)/σ, sample size n, and α. Increasing n is the main tool for simultaneously reducing both α and β. The simulation in Tab 3 shows how larger samples concentrate the sampling distribution, increasing the ability to detect small effects.

t-Distribution vs Normal

When σ is unknown and estimated by s, the test statistic T = (X̄ − μ₀)/(s/√n) follows a Student's t-distribution with df = n − 1 degrees of freedom, not a standard normal. The t-distribution has heavier tails than N(0,1), reflecting the additional uncertainty from estimating σ. As df → ∞, the t-distribution converges to N(0,1) — for n ≥ 30, the difference is practically negligible. William Sealy Gosset derived the t-distribution in 1908 while working at Guinness Brewery, publishing under the pseudonym "Student" to protect trade secrets.

One-Sample vs Two-Sample Tests

The one-sample t-test (this widget) compares a sample mean to a fixed null value. The two-sample t-test compares means from two independent groups. For two groups with possibly unequal variances, Welch's t-test uses a corrected degrees of freedom (the Welch-Satterthwaite equation). The paired t-test handles matched measurements (before/after) by reducing to a one-sample test on the differences — this eliminates between-subject variability and often yields higher power.
