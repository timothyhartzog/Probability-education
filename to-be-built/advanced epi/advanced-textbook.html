Chapter 11

Confounding & Stratified Analysis

The Mantel-Haenszel method for controlling confounders without regression

Confounding is the distortion of an exposure-disease association by a third variable that is associated with both the exposure and the outcome but does not lie on the causal pathway between them. It is arguably the single most important threat to the validity of observational epidemiological studies. Without identifying and controlling for confounders, we risk attributing to an exposure an effect that truly belongs to something else entirely.

Recognizing Confounding

A variable C is a confounder of the exposure-disease relationship if it meets three criteria simultaneously: C is associated with the exposure in the study population; C is an independent risk factor for the disease; and C is not on the causal pathway between exposure and disease (not an intermediate variable or mediator). A simple diagnostic: if adjusting for C changes the crude measure of association by more than 10%, confounding by C is likely present.

Key Concept: The 10% change-in-estimate rule is the standard criterion for identifying confounding. Compute the crude odds ratio (OR), then compute the adjusted (Mantel-Haenszel) OR. If |crude OR − adjusted OR| / adjusted OR > 10%, confounding is present and adjustment is necessary.

The Mantel-Haenszel Method

The Mantel-Haenszel (MH) method computes a weighted average of stratum-specific measures of association, effectively removing the confounding effect of the stratification variable. It is the most widely used method for controlling a single confounder without requiring regression modeling.

ORMH = Σ(aidi/ni) / Σ(bici/ni)

RRMH = Σ[ai(ci+di)/ni] / Σ[ci(ai+bi)/ni]

The MH chi-square statistic tests the null hypothesis that the common OR (or RR) equals 1 across all strata. The Breslow-Day test evaluates homogeneity — whether the stratum-specific ORs are similar enough to justify pooling into a single MH estimate. If the Breslow-Day test is significant, the stratum-specific ORs are heterogeneous, suggesting effect modification rather than simple confounding.

Rural Context: In rural epidemiology, confounders are often correlated with socioeconomic status, geographic access to care, occupational exposures (farming, mining), and age distribution. When you find an apparent association between a local environmental exposure and disease, always consider whether the age distribution or occupational mix in the exposed vs. unexposed groups might explain the finding. The Mantel-Haenszel method lets you stratify by these variables without needing large samples for regression.

Chapter 12

Effect Modification & Interaction

When the effect of exposure varies across subgroups

Effect modification (also called interaction or heterogeneity of effect) occurs when the magnitude of the effect of an exposure on an outcome varies across levels of a third variable. Unlike confounding — which is a bias to be eliminated — effect modification is a genuine biological or social phenomenon to be described and understood.

Distinguishing Confounding from Effect Modification

This is one of the most critical distinctions in epidemiology. Confounding produces a distorted overall estimate that can be "corrected" by adjustment; the adjusted estimate is the "true" effect. Effect modification produces genuinely different effects in different subgroups; a single pooled estimate is misleading because it masks important heterogeneity.

Clinical Pearl: The Breslow-Day test from the Mantel-Haenszel analysis is your primary statistical tool for detecting effect modification. If it is significant (p < 0.05), do NOT report a pooled MH estimate — instead, report stratum-specific estimates and describe the pattern of interaction. The clinical implication: the treatment or exposure may benefit one subgroup but harm another.

Additive vs. Multiplicative Interaction

Interaction can be assessed on the additive scale (differences in risk differences) or the multiplicative scale (ratios of risk ratios). These two scales can give different answers for the same data — interaction may be present on one scale but absent on the other. Public health decisions generally favor the additive scale because it directly measures excess cases; clinical decisions may use either.

Additive interaction: RERI = RR11 − RR10 − RR01 + 1
Multiplicative interaction: RR11 / (RR10 × RR01)

RERI (Relative Excess Risk due to Interaction) > 0 indicates positive interaction on the additive scale: the joint effect exceeds the sum of individual effects. This is the basis for the "sufficient cause" model in causal inference.

Rural Context: Effect modification is particularly relevant in rural health disparities research. An intervention that works well in urban settings may have a different effect size in rural populations due to differences in healthcare access, social support, transportation barriers, or comorbidity patterns. Always examine for effect modification by rural/urban status when applying urban-derived evidence to rural patients.
Chapter 13

Meta-Analysis & Forest Plots

Quantitative synthesis of evidence across studies

Meta-analysis is the statistical combination of results from two or more separate studies to produce a single pooled estimate of effect. It is the quantitative component of systematic review and sits at the top of the evidence hierarchy. For the rural provider, meta-analysis is the tool that transforms scattered, individually inconclusive small studies into clinically actionable evidence.

Fixed-Effect vs. Random-Effects Models

The fixed-effect model (inverse variance method) assumes all studies estimate the same true effect; differences are due to sampling error alone. Studies are weighted by the inverse of their variance (1/SE²) — larger, more precise studies get more weight.

The random-effects model (DerSimonian-Laird) assumes the true effect varies between studies. It adds a between-study variance component (τ²) to each study's weight, which has the effect of making the weights more uniform and giving relatively more influence to smaller studies. When heterogeneity is present, the random-effects CI is wider.

Fixed effect: θ̂F = Σ(wiθi) / Σ(wi), wi = 1/SEi²

Random effects: wi* = 1/(SEi² + τ²)
τ² = max(0, (Q − df) / C), C = Σw − Σw² / Σw

Heterogeneity Assessment

Cochran's Q tests whether the observed variation exceeds chance. I² quantifies the proportion of total variation due to heterogeneity rather than chance: I² < 25% = low, 25-75% = moderate, > 75% = high heterogeneity. When I² is high, the random-effects model should be preferred and sources of heterogeneity should be explored through subgroup analysis or meta-regression.

Clinical Pearl: When reading a meta-analysis, always check I². If I² > 50% and the studies include both urban and rural populations, ask whether rural-specific subgroup analysis was performed. A pooled estimate that combines urban and rural contexts may be misleading if the intervention effect differs systematically by setting.

Age Standardization

Direct and indirect methods for fair comparisons across populations

When comparing disease rates between two populations — say, your rural county vs. the state — differences in age distribution can profoundly distort the comparison. A county with a higher proportion of elderly residents will naturally have higher crude mortality, even if age-specific rates are identical. Standardization removes this confounding effect of age, producing rates that can be fairly compared.

Direct Standardization

Direct standardization applies the age-specific rates from the study population to a standard population's age distribution. The result is the rate that the study population would have if it had the same age structure as the standard population. It answers: "What would our county's mortality be if our age distribution matched the national standard?"

Direct Adjusted Rate = Σ(Ratei,study × Popi,standard) / Σ(Popi,standard)

Indirect Standardization & SMR

Indirect standardization applies the standard population's age-specific rates to the study population. The Standardized Mortality Ratio (SMR) or Standardized Incidence Ratio (SIR) compares observed events to expected events under the standard's rates. SMR > 1.0 means excess mortality; SMR < 1.0 means lower-than-expected mortality.

SMR = Observed events / Expected events
Expected = Σ(Ratei,standard × Popi,study)
Byar's 95% CI: O × (1 − 1/(9O) ∓ 1.96/(3√O))³ / E
Rural Context: The standard population used for age standardization dramatically affects results. The 2000 US Standard Population is most commonly used. Rural counties with aging populations (young adults emigrating for employment) often have misleadingly high crude rates that normalize after age adjustment. Conversely, if your county attracts retirees, crude rates may appear high even when age-specific health is excellent. Always present both crude and adjusted rates in community health assessments.

Chapter 15

ROC Curve Analysis

Receiver Operating Characteristic curves for optimizing diagnostic cutpoints

The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve is the definitive tool for evaluating diagnostic tests with continuous results. It plots sensitivity (y-axis) against 1 − specificity (x-axis) across all possible cutpoints, generating a curve whose shape reveals the test's discriminatory ability. The Area Under the Curve (AUC) provides a single summary statistic: AUC = 0.5 (no discrimination, equivalent to coin flip) to AUC = 1.0 (perfect discrimination).

AUC Interpretation

The AUC has an intuitive probabilistic interpretation: it is the probability that a randomly chosen diseased individual has a higher test value than a randomly chosen non-diseased individual. AUC guidelines: 0.5-0.6 = fail, 0.6-0.7 = poor, 0.7-0.8 = fair, 0.8-0.9 = good, 0.9-1.0 = excellent.

Optimal Cutpoint Selection

The Youden Index (J = Sensitivity + Specificity − 1) identifies the cutpoint that maximizes the vertical distance from the ROC curve to the diagonal line of no discrimination. However, this treats sensitivity and specificity as equally important. In clinical practice, the optimal cutpoint depends on the relative costs of false positives vs. false negatives — a screening test for cancer should favor high sensitivity (low false-negative rate) even at the cost of lower specificity.

Key Concept — Hanley-McNeil SE: The standard error of the AUC accounts for the correlation between sensitivity and specificity at different cutpoints. The Hanley-McNeil approximation uses: SE = √[(AUC(1-AUC) + (n₊-1)(Q₁-AUC²) + (n₋-1)(Q₂-AUC²)) / (n₊·n₋)], where Q₁ = AUC/(2-AUC) and Q₂ = 2AUC²/(1+AUC).

Bayesian Diagnostic Reasoning

Sequential test interpretation using pre-test and post-test probabilities

Bayesian diagnostic reasoning formalizes the clinical process of updating disease probability as new information becomes available. The pre-test probability (prevalence or clinical estimate) is updated to a post-test probability using the likelihood ratio of the test result. This framework is superior to simple PPV/NPV because it allows sequential updating with multiple tests and uses the clinician's prior probability estimate.

The Bayesian Update

Pre-test odds = Pre-test probability / (1 − Pre-test probability)
Post-test odds = Pre-test odds × Likelihood Ratio
Post-test probability = Post-test odds / (1 + Post-test odds)

For positive test: LR+ = Sensitivity / (1 − Specificity)
For negative test: LR− = (1 − Sensitivity) / Specificity

Sequential testing: after the first test, the post-test probability becomes the pre-test probability for the next test. This only works if the tests are conditionally independent given disease status — an assumption that is often violated in practice (e.g., two imaging tests of the same anatomy).

Clinical Pearl: In the ED, you perform Bayesian reasoning intuitively. A 25-year-old with atypical chest pain (low pre-test probability ~5%) gets a troponin. Even with a positive result (LR+ ≈ 10), the post-test probability is only ~35%. You need a second confirmatory test. For a 70-year-old diabetic with typical pain (pre-test ~80%), even a negative troponin (LR− ≈ 0.1) only drops the probability to ~30%. Dose-Response Modeling

Logistic regression for exposure-response relationships

The dose-response relationship is a cornerstone of causal reasoning in epidemiology and toxicology. If increasing doses of an exposure lead to increasing risk of disease in a graded, consistent fashion, this provides strong support for a causal interpretation. The logistic dose-response model — logit(p) = β₀ + β₁ × dose — is the standard parametric approach.

The Logistic Model

P(response) = 1 / (1 + exp(−(β₀ + β₁ × dose)))

LD₅₀ = −β₀/β₁ (dose producing 50% response)
LD₁₀ = −(β₀ + ln(9))/β₁ (dose for 10% response)

The model is fit using Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares (IRLS), equivalent to Newton-Raphson optimization of the log-likelihood. The Rust engine implements this with convergence tolerance of 10⁻⁸.

Rural Context: Dose-response analysis is directly relevant to environmental health investigations in rural settings — pesticide exposure and cancer risk, groundwater contamination levels and adverse birth outcomes, or occupational dust exposure and respiratory disease. If your community faces an environmental exposure concern, demonstrating (or refuting) a dose-response gradient is powerful evidence.

Chapter 18

Interrupted Time Series Analysis

Segmented regression for evaluating policy and intervention impacts

Interrupted Time Series (ITS) analysis is the strongest quasi-experimental design for evaluating the impact of an intervention or policy change when randomization is not possible. It requires a series of observations over time, with a known intervention point that divides the series into pre-intervention and post-intervention periods.

Segmented Regression Model

Yt = β₀ + β₁·T + β₂·D + β₃·P + εt

β₀ = baseline level, β₁ = pre-intervention trend
β₂ = level change at intervention, β₃ = slope change
Post-intervention slope = β₁ + β₃

The model captures four key features: the pre-intervention level (intercept), the pre-intervention trend (slope), the immediate change in level at the intervention point (β₂), and the change in trend after the intervention (β₃). The counterfactual — what would have happened without the intervention — is simply the extrapolation of the pre-intervention trend.

Clinical Pearl: ITS is ideal for evaluating quality improvement interventions at your hospital. Did implementing a new sepsis protocol change the trend in door-to-antibiotic times? Did a new handwashing campaign change hospital-acquired infection rates? You need at least 8-12 data points before and after the intervention for stable estimates. Monthly data over 2+ years is typical.

Monte Carlo Epidemic Simulation

Uncertainty quantification through stochastic parameter sweeps

Deterministic epidemic models (SIR/SEIR) produce a single trajectory for given parameter values. But in reality, epidemiological parameters are uncertain — R₀ may be estimated as 2.5 ± 0.5, the infectious period as 7 ± 2 days. Monte Carlo simulation addresses this by running hundreds or thousands of simulations with parameters randomly sampled from their uncertainty distributions, producing a distribution of possible outcomes rather than a single prediction.

Method

For each of N simulations: sample R₀ from Normal(μ, σ), sample infectious period from Normal(μ, σ), run the full SIR model with RK4 integration, and record key outcomes (peak infected, peak day, total infected). The collection of outcomes is then summarized by medians and percentiles (typically 5th and 95th) to create uncertainty bands.

Key Concept: Monte Carlo simulation converts parameter uncertainty into outcome uncertainty. Instead of saying "the peak will be 1,200 on day 45," you can say "the peak will be between 800 and 1,800 (90% interval), most likely around day 40-55." This honest uncertainty quantification is essential for pandemic planning: you must prepare for plausible worst cases, not just the median scenario.

Directed Acyclic Graphs & Causal Inference

Visual tools for identifying confounders, mediators, and colliders

Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) are the modern framework for causal reasoning in epidemiology. A DAG is a graph where nodes represent variables and directed edges (arrows) represent direct causal effects. "Acyclic" means no variable can cause itself through a chain of effects. DAGs make explicit the causal assumptions underlying a study and provide algorithmic rules for identifying which variables to adjust for and which to leave alone.

Key Structures

The Fork (Common Cause / Confounder)

X ← C → Y. The variable C causes both X and Y. Without adjustment, X and Y appear associated even if X does not cause Y. Solution: condition on (adjust for) C to close the backdoor path.

The Chain (Mediator)

X → M → Y. M lies on the causal pathway. Adjusting for M would block the causal effect of X on Y. Do NOT adjust for mediators unless you specifically want the "direct effect" of X on Y.

The Collider

X → C ← Y. Both X and Y cause C. By default, the path through C is blocked. Conditioning on C (or any descendant of C) opens the path and creates a spurious association between X and Y. This is "collider bias" or "selection bias."

Critical Warning — Collider Bias: Adjusting for a collider creates bias where none existed. This is counterintuitive and a common error. Example: if hospital admission (C) is caused by both severity of disease (X) and age (Y), then restricting analysis to hospitalized patients (conditioning on the collider) creates a spurious inverse association between disease severity and age. This "Berkson's paradox" is especially dangerous in case-series studies.
Rural Context: In rural epidemiology, access to care is a common collider. Both disease severity AND insurance status may determine who presents to your hospital. Analyzing only your patient population conditions on the collider "hospital presentation," potentially creating spurious associations. DAGs help identify when population-based data is needed vs. when hospital-based data introduces collider bias.
