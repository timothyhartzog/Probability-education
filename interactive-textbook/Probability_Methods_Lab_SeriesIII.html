Probability Integral TransformHow to generate any distribution from Uniform(0,1)

The PIT states: if U ~ Uniform(0,1) and F is any continuous CDF, then X = F⁻¹(U) has distribution F. This is the fundamental trick behind all random number generation. Watch uniform samples get "stretched" through the inverse CDF to produce exponential, normal, or any target distribution.

Animated Transform
Why It Matters
Every simulation library — NumPy, R, Julia — ultimately uses this transform (or variants like Box-Muller) to convert hardware-generated uniform bits into samples from any desired distribution.

Maximum Likelihood EstimationWatch the likelihood surface respond to each new data point

MLE finds the parameter values that make the observed data most probable. For a Normal(μ, σ²=1) model, the log-likelihood is ℓ(μ) = −n/2·log(2π) − Σ(xᵢ−μ)²/2. The MLE of μ is simply the sample mean. Click to add data points and watch the likelihood curve sharpen.

Kernel Density EstimationBuild a smooth PDF estimate from individual data kernels

KDE places a small "bump" (kernel) at each data point, then sums them. The bandwidth h controls smoothness: too small → spiky (overfitting); too large → oversmoothed (underfitting). This visualization shows each individual kernel plus their sum, compared to the true density.

Bandwidth Is Everything
Silverman's rule of thumb suggests h ≈ 1.06·σ·n^{−1/5}. But the optimal bandwidth depends on the true density shape — multimodal densities need smaller h to resolve the peaks.

Rejection SamplingAccept/reject: the universal algorithm for generating from complex distributions

To sample from a hard target density f(x), we sample from an easy proposal g(x) and accept with probability f(x)/(M·g(x)). Rejected points appear below the target curve. The acceptance rate = 1/M measures efficiency — tighter envelopes waste fewer samples.

Order StatisticsThe distribution of the minimum, maximum, median, and every rank in between

Given n i.i.d. samples, the k-th smallest value X₍ₖ₎ is the k-th order statistic. For Uniform(0,1) parents, X₍ₖ₎ ~ Beta(k, n−k+1). This explains why the sample median concentrates around the true median, and why extremes (min, max) have predictable distributions. Select any rank k to see its density.

Entropy & InformationMeasuring uncertainty across distributions

Shannon entropy H = −Σ p log p (discrete) or h = −∫ f log f dx (continuous differential entropy) quantifies how much uncertainty a distribution contains. Among all distributions on [0,∞) with fixed mean, the exponential has maximum entropy. Among all with fixed mean and variance, the normal wins. Compare entropies side by side.

Bootstrap ResamplingEstimating sampling distributions without parametric assumptions

The bootstrap draws B resamples (with replacement) from the observed data, computes a statistic on each, and uses the resulting distribution to estimate standard errors and confidence intervals. No distributional assumptions required. Watch the bootstrap distribution of the sample mean emerge from just n observations.

Bivariate Normal ExplorerContours, correlation, and conditional distributions

The bivariate normal is defined by means μ₁, μ₂, standard deviations σ₁, σ₂, and correlation ρ. Adjusting ρ tilts the elliptical contours. The conditional distribution X|Y=y is always normal — its mean shifts linearly with y, and its variance shrinks by factor (1−ρ²). This is the geometry behind linear regression.

Markov Chain SimulatorConvergence to stationary distribution and mixing

A Markov chain transitions between states with fixed probabilities. Under mild conditions, the chain converges to a stationary distribution π — no matter where it starts. Watch the empirical state frequencies converge to π as the chain runs. This is the foundation of MCMC methods.

Tail Comparison LabLog-scale view of how tails differ across distributions

On a log-scale y-axis, the tail behavior of distributions becomes strikingly clear. Normal tails drop off as exp(−x²) (parabolic on log-scale), while Student's t drops as a power law (linear on log-scale). Exponential tails are exactly linear. Pareto tails decline slowest. Tail behavior determines everything about extreme events.

Log-Scale Tails
Why Tails Matter
In risk management, the difference between normal and t(3) tails means the difference between a "once in 10,000 years" event and a "once a decade" event. Underestimating tail risk is the root cause of many financial catastrophes.

Sufficient StatisticsWhy the sample mean captures ALL the information about μ

A statistic T(X) is sufficient for parameter θ if the conditional distribution of X given T doesn't depend on θ. For Normal(μ,1), the sample mean X̄ is sufficient for μ — knowing X̄, the individual observations provide zero additional information about μ. This demonstration shows that two datasets with the same X̄ produce identical likelihood functions.

Fisher–Neyman Factorization
T is sufficient for θ iff the joint density factors as f(x|θ) = g(T(x), θ) · h(x). The h(x) part doesn't depend on θ — so once T is known, the data tell you nothing more about the parameter.
