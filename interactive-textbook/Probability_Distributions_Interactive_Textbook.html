Part I — Mathematical Foundations

Chapter 1: Probability Spaces & Measure Theory

1.1 Sample Spaces and Events

A probability model begins with a sample space Ω — the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. An event is a subset of Ω. For continuous sample spaces (such as ℝ), we use σ-algebras to ensure probabilities are well-defined.

A σ-algebra ℱ on Ω is a collection of subsets satisfying: (i) Ω ∈ ℱ, (ii) closure under complements, and (iii) closure under countable unions. The triple (Ω, ℱ, P) is a probability space — the rigorous foundation of all probability theory.

1.2 Kolmogorov's Axioms

Andrey Kolmogorov's 1933 axioms define a probability measure P on (Ω, ℱ): normalization P(Ω)=1, non-negativity P(A) ≥ 0, and countable additivity for disjoint sets. From these three axioms, the entire edifice of probability theory is derived.

Key Consequences

P(∅) = 0; monotonicity (A ⊆ B ⟹ P(A) ≤ P(B)); inclusion-exclusion: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B); complement rule: P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A).

1.3 Conditional Probability & Bayes' Theorem

For events A and B with P(B) > 0, the conditional probability P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B)/P(B). Bayes' theorem follows: P(A|B) = P(B|A)·P(A)/P(B). This is the mathematical engine behind Bayesian inference, clinical diagnostics, and machine learning.

1.4 Random Variables

A random variable X: Ω → ℝ maps outcomes to real numbers. Its CDF F(x) = P(X ≤ x) fully characterizes its distribution. Discrete random variables have a PMF p(x) = P(X = x); continuous random variables have a PDF f(x) where F(x) = ∫f(t)dt.

1.5 Expectation, Variance, and Moments

The expectation E[X] measures central tendency. The variance Var(X) = E[(X−μ)²] measures spread. Higher moments include skewness (asymmetry, via the 3rd central moment) and kurtosis (tail heaviness, via the 4th).

1.6 Moment-Generating Functions

The MGF M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] uniquely determines a distribution (when it exists). The k-th moment is the k-th derivative at t=0. For independent variables: M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t)·M_Y(t). The characteristic function φ_X(t) = E[e^{itX}] always exists and is used in proofs of the CLT.

1.7 Probability Inequalities

Fundamental Inequalities

Markov: P(X ≥ a) ≤ E[X]/a for non-negative X. Chebyshev: P(|X−μ| ≥ kσ) ≤ 1/k². Jensen: E[g(X)] ≥ g(E[X]) for convex g.

Part II — Discrete Probability Distributions

Chapter 2: Bernoulli & Binomial Distributions

2.1 The Bernoulli Distribution

The simplest nontrivial distribution: a single trial with success probability p. P(X=x) = p^x(1−p)^{1−x} for x ∈ {0,1}. Named after Jacob Bernoulli (1654–1705). Mean = p, Variance = p(1−p).

2.2 The Binomial Distribution

The number of successes X in n independent Bernoulli(p) trials. The PMF is:

P(X = k) = C(n,k) · p^k · (1-p)^{n-k}, k = 0, 1, ..., n

Mean = np, Variance = np(1−p), MGF = (q + pe^t)^n. Connected to the Poisson (as n→∞ with np=λ fixed) and the Normal (for large n via CLT).

Chapter 3: Geometric & Negative Binomial

3.1 The Geometric Distribution

Models the number of trials until the first success. P(X=k) = (1−p)^{k−1}·p for k=1,2,3,... Mean = 1/p, Variance = (1−p)/p². The geometric distribution is the unique discrete distribution with the memoryless property: P(X > s+t | X > s) = P(X > t).

3.2 The Negative Binomial Distribution

Generalizes the geometric to model trials until the r-th success. Also serves as a gamma-Poisson mixture for overdispersed count data. Widely used in genomics (RNA-seq), epidemiology, and insurance.

P(X = k) = C(k-1, r-1) · p^r · (1-p)^{k-r}, k = r, r+1, ...

Chapter 4: The Poisson Distribution

Models the number of events in a fixed interval when events occur independently at a constant average rate λ. Named after Siméon Denis Poisson (1781–1840).

P(X = k) = e^{−λ} · λ^k / k!, k = 0, 1, 2, ...

A defining property: mean = variance = λ. The Poisson arises as the limit of Binomial(n, p) when n→∞ and np→λ (Poisson limit theorem / law of rare events). The reproductive property: independent Poisson sums are Poisson.

Chapter 5: The Hypergeometric Distribution

Models sampling without replacement from a finite population of N items, K of which are successes. Drawing a sample of size n, the number of successes X has PMF:

P(X = k) = C(K,k) · C(N−K, n−k) / C(N, n)

The finite population correction factor (N−n)/(N−1) distinguishes this from the binomial. Used in Fisher's exact test, quality acceptance sampling, and capture-recapture ecology.

Chapter 6: Discrete Uniform & Multinomial

6.1 Discrete Uniform

Equal probability 1/(b−a+1) to each integer in {a, …, b}. The maximum-entropy distribution on a finite set — representing maximum uncertainty. A fair die is DiscreteUniform(1,6).

6.2 Multinomial

Generalizes the binomial to k outcomes per trial. In n trials with outcome probabilities p₁,…,pₖ, the joint PMF involves the multinomial coefficient n!/(x₁!···xₖ!). Each marginal Xᵢ ~ Bin(n, pᵢ); the covariance Cov(Xᵢ,Xⱼ) = −npᵢpⱼ is always negative (counts must sum to n).

Part III — Continuous Probability Distributions

Chapter 7: Continuous Uniform Distribution

The simplest continuous distribution: f(x) = 1/(b−a) on [a,b]. The probability integral transform — if U ~ Uniform(0,1), then F⁻¹(U) has CDF F — is the basis for simulating any continuous distribution from a uniform RNG.

Chapter 8: The Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

Unquestionably the most important distribution in statistics. Discovered by de Moivre (~1733), independently derived by Laplace and Gauss.

f(x) = (1/(σ√(2π))) · exp(−(x−μ)² / (2σ²))
The 68–95–99.7 Rule

~68% of probability within ±1σ of the mean, ~95% within ±2σ, ~99.7% within ±3σ. The normal distribution has maximum differential entropy among all distributions with given mean and variance.

8.4 The Central Limit Theorem

The CLT is arguably the most important theorem in statistics: the standardized sum of i.i.d. random variables with finite variance converges to N(0,1). This explains why the normal distribution appears so pervasively — whenever a quantity results from many small, independent, additive effects.

Chapter 9: Exponential & Gamma Distributions

9.1 The Exponential Distribution

The continuous analogue of the geometric distribution — models waiting time until the first event in a Poisson process. f(x) = λe^{−λx} for x ≥ 0. Mean = 1/λ, Variance = 1/λ². Has the unique continuous memoryless property and constant hazard rate h(t) = λ.

9.2 The Gamma Distribution

Generalizes the exponential to model the wait until the α-th event. Shape parameter α and rate parameter β give mean = α/β and variance = α/β². Special cases include the Exponential (α=1), Chi-squared (α=ν/2, β=1/2), and Erlang (α ∈ ℤ⁺).

Chapter 10: The Beta Distribution

Defined on [0,1], the natural distribution for proportions and probabilities. Extremely flexible — symmetric bell, U-shaped, left-skewed, or right-skewed depending on parameters α and β.

f(x) = [Γ(α+β)/(Γ(α)Γ(β))] · x^{α-1} · (1-x)^{β-1}, 0 < x < 1

The conjugate prior for the Bernoulli/binomial parameter p: if prior is Beta(α,β) and we observe k successes in n trials, the posterior is Beta(α+k, β+n−k). Also the distribution of order statistics from Uniform(0,1).

Chapter 11: Weibull, Log-Normal & Other Distributions

11.1 The Weibull Distribution

The standard model for failure-time analysis. Shape k controls the hazard: k=1 gives constant hazard (exponential), k>1 models wear-out failures, k<1 models infant mortality. Survival function S(x) = exp(−(x/λ)^k).

11.2 The Log-Normal Distribution

If ln(X) ~ N(μ,σ²), then X is log-normal. Always right-skewed with heavier tails than the normal. Models incomes (Gibrat's law), stock prices (Black-Scholes), particle sizes, hospital lengths of stay, and many biological measurements.

11.3 The Cauchy Distribution

The prototype heavy-tailed distribution. f(x) = 1/(π(1+x²)). No finite moments exist — the mean, variance, and all higher moments are undefined. The law of large numbers fails for Cauchy random variables. Arises as Z₁/Z₂ where both are standard normal, and equals Student's t with 1 degree of freedom.

11.4 The Pareto Distribution

Models the "80/20 rule" — power-law tails. f(x) = αx_m^α / x^{α+1} for x ≥ x_m. Used for income, city sizes (Zipf's law), internet file sizes, and insurance claims. When α ≤ 1, even the mean is infinite.

Part IV — Multivariate Distributions

Chapter 12: Joint, Marginal & Conditional Distributions

For two random variables X and Y, the joint CDF F(x,y) = P(X ≤ x, Y ≤ y) captures their full joint behavior. Marginals are obtained by integrating out the other variable. Independence ⟺ f(x,y) = f_X(x)·f_Y(y).

12.2 Covariance & Correlation

Cov(X,Y) = E[XY] − E[X]E[Y]. The Pearson correlation ρ = Cov(X,Y)/(σ_X·σ_Y) satisfies −1 ≤ ρ ≤ 1. Independence implies ρ=0, but ρ=0 does not imply independence (except for the multivariate normal).

12.3 The Multivariate Normal

The most important multivariate distribution. X ~ N(μ, Σ) has the special property that uncorrelated components are independent. Every linear combination is univariate normal. Foundational for PCA, discriminant analysis, Gaussian processes, and variational inference.

Part V — Convergence & Limit Theorems

Chapter 13: Modes of Convergence

Four modes form a hierarchy: Almost sure and Mean-square convergence each imply convergence in probability, which implies convergence in distribution (the weakest, relevant to the CLT).

Chapter 14: Law of Large Numbers & CLT

The Weak LLN (Khinchin, 1929): sample means converge in probability to the population mean. The Strong LLN (Kolmogorov, 1933): almost sure convergence. These justify the frequentist interpretation of probability and the consistency of statistical estimators.

The Berry-Esseen theorem bounds the rate of CLT convergence: the maximum CDF deviation from Φ is at most C·E[|X₁−μ|³]/(σ³√n) with C ≤ 0.4748. The Delta method extends the CLT to smooth functions of sample means.

Part VI — Applications

Chapter 15: Clinical Medicine & Public Health

Diagnostic Testing: Sensitivity, specificity, and Bayes' theorem determine PPV — with profound implications for low-prevalence screening. Survival Analysis: Exponential (constant hazard), Weibull (monotone hazard), and log-normal models underlie Kaplan-Meier estimation and Cox regression. Epidemiology: Poisson models for incidence, negative binomial for overdispersed infectious disease data, binomial for clinical trial outcomes.

Chapter 16: Engineering & Quality Management

Reliability: The bathtub curve uses Weibull distributions with different shape parameters for infant mortality, useful life, and wear-out phases. SPC: X-bar charts (normal via CLT), c-charts (Poisson for defect counts), p-charts (binomial for proportions). Six Sigma and CMS quality reporting are deeply rooted in distribution theory.

Chapter 17: Finance & Economics

Asset Returns: Log-normal models (Black-Scholes) underestimate tail risk; Student's t and stable distributions better capture empirical heavy tails. Risk: VaR and Expected Shortfall are quantile-based measures. Queueing: M/M/1 queues (Poisson arrivals, exponential service) have geometric steady-state queue lengths — applicable to ED patient flow and server load balancing.

Chapter 18: Machine Learning & Data Science

Generative Models: GMMs use multivariate normals; LDA uses multinomial/Dirichlet; VAEs use normal latent spaces. Bayesian Inference: Conjugate families (beta-binomial, gamma-Poisson, normal-normal) yield closed-form posteriors; non-conjugate models use MCMC or variational methods. Hypothesis Testing: t, F, χ², and z statistics all derive from the normal distribution family.
