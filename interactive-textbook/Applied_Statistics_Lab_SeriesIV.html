A hypothesis test compares a null hypothesis H₀: μ=μ₀ against an alternative H₁: μ=μ₁. The significance level α sets the false positive rate (Type I error). The Tradeoff
Reducing α (fewer false positives) always increases β (more false negatives) unless you increase sample size. The only way to simultaneously lower both is to collect more data — reducing σ/√n.

p-Value MisconceptionsWhat the p-value is — and what it absolutely is not

The p-value is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one computed, assuming H₀ is true. It is NOT the probability that H₀ is true. This simulator generates thousands of experiments under both true and false nulls to show the distribution of p-values in each scenario.

p-Value Distribution
0.0
20
Key Insight
When H₀ is true (effect = 0), p-values are uniformly distributed on [0,1] — exactly 5% fall below 0.05 by design. When a real effect exists, p-values pile up near zero. This is why a single p-value tells you very little without context.

MCMC Metropolis-Hastings SamplerRandom walk that converges to any target distribution

Metropolis-Hastings generates a Markov chain whose stationary distribution equals the target. At each step: propose x' from a symmetric proposal, accept with probability min(1, f(x')/f(x)). The chain explores the target density — watch the histogram fill in the target shape.

MCMC Trace
1.0
Proposal Width Matters
Too small σ → high acceptance but slow exploration (random walk). Too large → many rejections, chain gets stuck. The optimal acceptance rate for a random-walk Metropolis is roughly 23% (Roberts et al. 1997).

Birthday ProblemWhy 23 people give you a 50% chance of a shared birthday

In a room of n people, the probability of at least one shared birthday is P = 1 − 365!/((365−n)!·365ⁿ). Human intuition dramatically underestimates this because we think about specific pairs, not all possible pairs. With 23 people there are C(23,2) = 253 pairs — and 50% of the time, at least one pair matches.

Monty Hall SimulatorShould you switch? Simulate 10,000 games and see.

Behind three doors: one car, two goats. You pick a door. Monty (who knows what's behind them) opens a goat door. Should you switch? Intuition says 50/50, but switching wins 2/3 of the time. Your initial pick had 1/3 probability; the remaining 2/3 collapses onto the other unopened door.

Copulas & DependenceBeyond correlation — how variables can be dependent without being linearly related

A copula separates the marginal distributions from the dependence structure. Two variables can have identical marginals and identical correlation but vastly different joint behavior — especially in the tails. The Gaussian copula has symmetric tail dependence (none), while the Clayton copula concentrates dependence in the lower tail (joint crashes).

Joint Scatter
2.0
Why Copulas Matter
The 2008 financial crisis was partly blamed on overreliance on the Gaussian copula for pricing CDOs — it dramatically underestimated the probability of joint defaults (lower tail dependence). Clayton or Student-t copulas would have warned of much higher systemic risk.
