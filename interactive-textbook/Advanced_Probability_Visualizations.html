Galton Board — The CLT in ActionWatch individual Bernoulli trials aggregate into a binomial → normal distribution

Sir Francis Galton's "bean machine" is the physical embodiment of the Central Limit Theorem. Each ball makes n independent left/right decisions (Bernoulli trials). The sum of these decisions — a Binomial(n, 0.5) random variable — converges to a normal distribution as n grows. Watch the histogram emerge in real time.

The Galton board demonstrates that the sum of many independent binary choices produces a bell curve — the essence of the CLT. When p ≠ 0.5, the distribution shifts and becomes skewed for small n, but still converges to normal as n grows.

Convolution VisualizerSee what happens when you add independent random variables

The distribution of X+Y (for independent X, Y) is the convolution of their individual distributions. This is the mathematical engine behind the CLT: adding more and more variables makes the sum approach normality. Stack up to 8 distributions and watch the convolved result evolve.

Memoryless PropertyWhy the exponential "forgets" how long it has waited

The exponential distribution has a unique property: P(X > s+t | X > s) = P(X > t). Given that no event has occurred in the first s time units, the probability of waiting at least t more is exactly the same as the unconditional probability. This visualization compares the conditional survival with the unconditional — they're identical.

Contrast — Weibull Is Not Memoryless
Toggle to a Weibull(k>1): the conditional survival curve shifts below the unconditional — having waited longer makes failure more imminent (increasing hazard). Only the exponential's constant hazard produces memorylessness.

QQ-Plot ExplorerThe most powerful visual normality diagnostic

A QQ (quantile-quantile) plot compares the quantiles of your sample against the quantiles of a theoretical distribution. If the data follow that distribution, points fall on the diagonal. Deviations reveal skewness, heavy tails, or distributional mismatch. Generate samples from different distributions and see how the QQ plot detects non-normality.

Hazard Function LaboratoryThe bathtub curve and failure rate modeling

The hazard function h(t) = f(t)/S(t) gives the instantaneous failure rate at time t, given survival to t. The "bathtub curve" combines: infant mortality (decreasing hazard, Weibull k<1), useful life (constant hazard, exponential), and wear-out (increasing hazard, Weibull k>1). Compare four distributions simultaneously.

Confidence Interval CoverageWhat "95% confidence" really means

A 95% confidence interval does not mean "95% probability the parameter is in this interval." It means: if we repeat the experiment many times, ~95% of the computed intervals will contain the true parameter. Watch 100 intervals — ~5 will miss.
